Sierra Leone

The “Lion Mountains” — History, Politics & Current Events

West AfricaRepublic since 1971Pop. ~9 million
Hi everyone. My presentation is on Sierra Leone, a small country on the west coast of Africa. Its name comes from Portuguese sailors who called it the "Lion Mountains." Over the next fifteen minutes I'll walk through three things: its history — which is deeply tied to slavery and to a brutal civil war — its politics today, and the big current events happening there right now, in 2025 and 2026. As you'll see, it's a country of hard history but also real hope.

First, let me orient you. Sierra Leone sits on the Atlantic coast of West Africa, next to Guinea and Liberia. The capital is Freetown. About nine million people live there, and it's a very young country — the median age is only about twenty, which matters for almost everything we'll talk about. English is the official language, but almost everyone speaks Krio, a local language you'll hear about in a minute. It's a mostly Muslim country with a Christian minority, and it's actually famous for how peacefully the two religions live together.
History · before 1462

Before the Europeans arrived

  • People have lived here for thousands of years — farming, fishing, and working iron.
  • Long-established groups include the Temne, Mende, Limba, and the coastal Bullom/Sherbro.
  • Many arrived through migrations from the interior; the coast sat at the western edge of West Africa’s great trade networks in the era of the Mali Empire.
  • Powerful traditional “secret societies” — the Poro (men) and Sande/Bondo (women) — ran initiation schools and still shape culture today.

Carved soapstone “nomoli” figures, made before Portuguese contact, are still found across the country.

Nomoli soapstone figure · photo Ji-Elle, CC BY-SA · Wikimedia Commons
Before any Europeans showed up, this land already had a long history. People had lived here for thousands of years, farming, fishing along the coast, and working iron. Several of today's major groups were already well established — the Temne, the Mende, the Limba, and the coastal Bullom and Sherbro peoples. Many of them had migrated in from the interior, and the region sat at the western edge of the huge trade networks of West Africa during the time of the powerful Mali Empire. Society was organized partly through what are called "secret societies" — the Poro for men and the Sande for women — which ran initiation schools and still influence the culture today. One cool detail: carved soapstone figures called "nomoli," made before the Portuguese ever arrived, are still dug up by farmers across the country. So this was a settled, organized society long before 1462.
History · 1462–1800s

Named by explorers, scarred by slavery

  • Portuguese navigator Pedro de Sintra mapped the coast c. 1462 and named it “Serra Leoa” — Lion Mountains. (exact origin debated)
  • Bunce Island, up the Freetown river, became a British slave-trading castle.
  • Its captives were shipped especially to South Carolina & Georgia — a direct link to the Gullah people of the American South.
Bunce Island, 1805 engraving · Wikimedia Commons
The name Sierra Leone goes back to about 1462, when a Portuguese explorer named the mountains behind the coast the "Lion Mountains." But the darker part of the early history is slavery. On a small island in the river near Freetown, the British ran a slave-trading fort called Bunce Island. Here's the connection to us: many of the people sold from Bunce Island were shipped to South Carolina and Georgia, because they knew how to grow rice. Their descendants are the Gullah people of the American South. So Sierra Leone's history is literally part of American history.
History · impact & legacy

Slavery’s long shadow — and a fight for freedom

Sengbe Pieh (Joseph Cinqué), N. Jocelyn 1839 · public domain, Wikimedia Commons
  • Bunce Island shipped tens of thousands of captives across the Atlantic — a chief supplier to the rice plantations of South Carolina & Georgia.
  • Traders called this the “Rice Coast”: planters paid premium prices for people whose rice-growing skill helped build the Lowcountry economy.
  • Their descendants, the Gullah/Geechee of the American South, still keep West African foods, words, and a creole language linked to Krio — and in the 1980s Gullah families traveled “home” to Bunce Island.
  • In 1839, a Mende man — Sengbe Pieh (Joseph Cinqué) — led the revolt on the Amistad; the U.S. Supreme Court freed the captives (1841) and he returned home a hero.

Across four centuries, ~12.5 million Africans were forced onto slave ships; about 10.7 million survived the Middle Passage.

I want to spend a moment on how deeply the slave trade shaped Sierra Leone, because its impact reaches all the way to the United States. From Bunce Island, tens of thousands of people were shipped across the Atlantic, and it was a major supplier to the rice plantations of South Carolina and Georgia. Slave traders actually called this part of Africa the "Rice Coast," and planters paid extra for people from here because they knew how to grow rice — that expertise helped build the whole Lowcountry economy. Those captives' descendants are the Gullah, or Geechee, people of the American South. To this day they keep West African foods, words, and a language connected to Sierra Leone's Krio — and in the 1980s, Gullah families actually traveled "home" to Bunce Island for an emotional reunion. And here's a story you might know from the movie: in 1839 a man from Sierra Leone named Sengbe Pieh, also called Joseph Cinqué, led a revolt on a slave ship called the Amistad. Their case went all the way to the U.S. Supreme Court, which set them free in 1841, and he returned home a hero. To put the scale in perspective: over about four centuries, roughly twelve and a half million Africans were forced onto slave ships, and only about ten and a half million survived the journey.
History · 1787–1808

Freetown: a home for freed people

Freetown today · Wikimedia Commons
  • 1787: British abolitionists found the “Province of Freedom” for formerly enslaved people.
  • Settled by London’s “Black Poor,” Black Loyalists from Nova Scotia, and Jamaican Maroons.
  • The Royal Navy freed captives from slave ships — the “Recaptives” — and resettled them here.
  • Together they became the Krio people, whose language unites the country today.

The circle of history: a slave castle and a “City of Freedom” sit on the same river.

Now the remarkable twist. Just twenty miles from that slave castle, in 1787, British abolitionists founded a settlement for freed slaves. They literally called it the "Province of Freedom," and the city became Freetown. It filled up with formerly enslaved people from London, Black Loyalists resettled from Nova Scotia in Canada, and Jamaican Maroons. Then, after Britain banned the slave trade, the navy would stop slave ships and free the captives — called "Recaptives" — right here. All these groups blended into a new people called the Krio, and their language, also called Krio, is what unites the whole country today. So on one river you have both a symbol of slavery and a symbol of freedom.
History · culture · from 1827

The “Athens of West Africa”

  • Fourah Bay College, founded in Freetown in 1827, was the first Western-style college in West Africa.
  • In 1876 it affiliated with England’s Durham University and began awarding degrees, drawing students from across the region.
  • So many schools and scholars clustered here that Freetown earned the nickname “the Athens of West Africa.”
  • It educated a Krio elite — clergy, teachers, doctors, and lawyers — who then served across British West Africa.

Its first student, Samuel Ajayi Crowther, became the first African Anglican bishop.

Old Fourah Bay College building, Freetown · CC BY 2.0 · Wikimedia Commons
Here's something Sierra Leone was once famous for that surprises people: education. In 1827, missionaries founded Fourah Bay College right here in Freetown — the first Western-style college anywhere in West Africa. In 1876 it linked up with Durham University in England and began granting real university degrees, which drew ambitious students from all over the region. There were so many schools and educated people in Freetown that it earned a proud nickname: "the Athens of West Africa," after the ancient Greek city famous for learning. The college trained a whole class of Krio professionals — priests, teachers, doctors, lawyers — who then went to work across Britain's West African colonies. Its very first student, Samuel Ajayi Crowther, went on to become the first African Anglican bishop. So long before the war, Sierra Leone was a center of learning for the whole region.
History · 1808–1961

Colony, resistance, independence

  • Britain ran coastal Freetown as a Crown Colony (1808) and declared the interior a Protectorate (1896) — a split that shaped later politics.
  • 1898 Hut Tax War: chief Bai Bureh led armed resistance to British taxes — an early hero of anti-colonial defiance.
  • 27 April 1961: independence from Britain. Sir Milton Margai became the first Prime Minister.
Bai Bureh, 1898 · Wikimedia Commons
For most of the 1800s and 1900s, Britain ruled. It governed the coast around Freetown directly, but the inland areas only indirectly, through local chiefs — and that division caused tension for decades. In 1898, when the British tried to tax people's homes — the "hut tax" — a northern chief named Bai Bureh led an armed revolt. He lost, but he's remembered as an early hero who stood up to colonial rule. Finally, on the 27th of April, 1961, Sierra Leone became independent, with Sir Milton Margai as its first Prime Minister. Independence Day is still celebrated every April 27th.
History · 1968–1985

From democracy to one-party rule

Siaka Stevens · Wikimedia Commons
  • Siaka Stevens (APC party) took power and made Sierra Leone a republic in 1971.
  • A 1978 vote made his party the only legal party — a one-party state.
  • Years of corruption and economic decline hollowed out the government.

These weaknesses set the stage for the civil war that followed.

Independence started with hope, but it didn't last. A politician named Siaka Stevens took power and, in 1971, turned the country into a republic with himself as president. By 1978 he had banned all other parties — so Sierra Leone became a one-party state. His long rule was marked by heavy corruption and a collapsing economy. The government basically stopped working for ordinary people. Remember this, because that broken, corrupt system is exactly what led to the horror of the next slide — the civil war.
History · 1991–2002

The civil war: blood diamonds & child soldiers

  • In 1991, the rebel RUF, led by Foday Sankoh and backed by Liberia’s Charles Taylor, invaded.
  • Rebels funded the war by selling “blood diamonds” mined by force.
  • They used drugged child soldiers and committed mass atrocities.
  • Roughly 50,000–70,000 killed; about 2.5 million displaced — half the country.
Diamond mining, Sierra Leone · Wikimedia Commons
In 1991, civil war broke out, and it was one of the most brutal wars of the twentieth century. A rebel group called the RUF, led by Foday Sankoh and backed by the warlord Charles Taylor from neighboring Liberia, invaded the country. They paid for the war by seizing diamond mines and selling what became known as "blood diamonds." This is where that term comes from. The RUF became infamous for two things: forcing children to become soldiers, and cutting off civilians' hands and arms. Somewhere between fifty and seventy thousand people were killed, and about two and a half million — half the population — were forced from their homes.
History · 2000–2012

How it ended — and justice

Charles Taylor · Wikimedia Commons
  • West African (ECOMOG) and UN peacekeepers, plus a decisive British intervention in 2000, ended the war (2002).
  • A Special Court convicted Liberia’s Charles Taylor in 2012 — 50 years in prison.
  • He was the first former head of state convicted by an international court since Nuremberg.
  • A Truth & Reconciliation Commission helped the country heal.
So how did it end? Outside help was crucial. West African peacekeepers and a large UN mission fought the rebels, and in the year 2000 British troops stepped in and decisively turned the tide. The war was declared over in 2002. Afterward came justice. A special international court put the leaders on trial. Its most famous conviction was Charles Taylor, the Liberian president who had backed the rebels — he got fifty years in prison. That made him the first former head of state convicted by an international court since the Nuremberg trials after World War Two. Sierra Leone also created a Truth and Reconciliation Commission, where victims and even perpetrators told their stories to help the country move forward.
History · 2014–2016

Ebola: a fragile recovery tested

  • Just over a decade after the war, the largest Ebola outbreak in history struck the region.
  • Sierra Leone: roughly 14,000 cases and ~4,000 deaths.
  • The epidemic overwhelmed a health system still weak from war — and killed many of the country’s doctors and nurses.

A recurring theme: each crisis lands on a system with little to spare.

Humanitarian response · Wikimedia Commons
The country was just getting back on its feet when the next disaster hit. In 2014, the worst Ebola outbreak in history swept through West Africa. In Sierra Leone alone there were about fourteen thousand cases and around four thousand deaths. The tragedy was that the health system was still weak from the war, so it was quickly overwhelmed — and Ebola killed many of the very doctors and nurses the country couldn't afford to lose. You'll notice a pattern here: whenever a crisis hits, it lands on a system that has almost no cushion. Keep that in mind as we move into the present day.
Politics · Today

How the government works

President Julius Maada Bio · Wikimedia Commons
  • A constitutional presidential republic under the 1991 Constitution, with an elected one-chamber Parliament.
  • The president is both head of state and government — two 5-year terms max.
  • Current president: Julius Maada Bio (elected 2018, re-elected 2023); next election 2028.
  • Two big parties: SLPP (south/east) vs. APC (north) — split largely along ethnic and regional lines.
Now to politics today. Sierra Leone is a democratic republic with a written constitution from 1991. It has an elected parliament that makes laws, and a president who is both the head of state and the head of the government — like combining a king and a prime minister into one elected office. Presidents can serve at most two five-year terms. The current president is Julius Maada Bio, first elected in 2018 and re-elected in 2023. The next election is in 2028. Two parties dominate: the SLPP, strong in the south and east, and the APC, strong in the north. And here's the key thing — people mostly vote based on their region and ethnic group, not on ideas. That makes elections very tense, which brings us to the next slide.
Politics · Structure

Three branches of government

House of Parliament, Freetown · CC BY 2.0 · Wikimedia Commons
  • Executive — the President (head of state & government), a Vice President, and a Cabinet of ministers.
  • Legislative — a one-chamber Parliament of 149 seats (135 elected members + 14 paramount chiefs), led by a Speaker.
  • Judiciary — independent courts topped by the Supreme Court, headed by the Chief Justice.
  • Local level — elected district & local councils work alongside traditional paramount chiefs.

All three branches are set out in the 1991 Constitution, with power separated among them.

Let me break down how that government is actually structured, because — like the United States — it has three branches, all laid out in the 1991 constitution. First, the executive branch: that's the President, who leads the country, plus a Vice President and a Cabinet of ministers who run the different departments. Second, the legislative branch: a single-chamber Parliament with 149 seats. Most of those — 135 — are elected by the people, and 14 special seats are reserved for traditional "paramount chiefs." Parliament is run by a Speaker and it makes the laws. Third, the judiciary — the courts — which are independent, with the Supreme Court at the top, led by the Chief Justice. And below the national level, there are elected local councils that share power with the traditional chiefs. So it mixes a modern democracy with older traditional leadership.
Politics · Administrative divisions

How the country is divided

  • Five regions: four provinces — Eastern, Northern, North West, and Southern — plus the Western Area, home to Freetown.
  • 16 districts — two new ones, Karene and Falaba, were added in a 2017 reorganization.
  • Districts break down into about 190 chiefdoms, each led by a paramount chief.
  • The Western Area (the capital region) is split into Urban (Freetown) and Rural.
The 16 districts · map NordNordWest, CC BY-SA · Wikimedia Commons
Here's how the country is organized on a map. At the top level there are five regions: four provinces — the Eastern, Northern, North West, and Southern provinces — plus the Western Area, which is where the capital, Freetown, sits. Those regions are divided into 16 districts. That number used to be 14, but in 2017 the government reorganized things and added two new districts, Karene and Falaba. Below the districts are smaller units called chiefdoms — there are about a hundred and ninety of them, each led by a traditional paramount chief. And the Western Area around Freetown is special: instead of chiefdoms it's just split into an Urban part, the city itself, and a Rural part around it.
Politics · 2023–2024

A fragile democracy

  • 2023 election: Bio re-elected, but the Carter Center and EU observers flagged serious problems counting the votes.
  • Nov 2023: an attempted coup — gunmen attacked an armory and broke open a prison in Freetown.
  • Ex-president Ernest Bai Koroma was charged with treason, then went into exile in Nigeria.
  • Rated “Partly Free” — but press freedom is rising and corruption scores are holding steady.
Ernest Bai Koroma · Wikimedia Commons
Sierra Leone's democracy is real, but fragile. In the 2023 election, President Bio won re-election, but international observers — including the Carter Center, founded by former US President Jimmy Carter, and the European Union — said the vote-counting had serious problems and wasn't transparent. Then, just months later, in November 2023, there was an attempted coup: armed men attacked a military weapons store and broke thousands of prisoners out of jail in the capital. A former president, Ernest Bai Koroma, was even charged with treason and ended up in exile in Nigeria. So watchdog groups rate the country only "Partly Free." But — and this is important — its press freedom has genuinely been rising, and its corruption score is holding steady rather than getting worse. It's a mixed picture, not a simple one.
Politics · 2021–2025

Punching above its weight — and reforming

National coat of arms · Wikimedia Commons

On the world stage

  • Held a seat on the UN Security Council (2024–25).
  • President Bio elected chairman of ECOWAS, West Africa’s regional bloc (2025).

Progressive reforms

  • Abolished the death penalty (2021).
  • 30% of government jobs reserved for women (2022).
  • Banned child marriage (2024) — though ending FGM is the next fight.
Now for the good news, because there's a lot of it. For a small, poor country, Sierra Leone has real influence abroad. It recently held a seat on the UN Security Council, and in 2025 President Bio was chosen to chair ECOWAS, the group of West African nations — basically West Africa's top diplomatic job. At home, the government has passed some genuinely progressive reforms: it abolished the death penalty in 2021; it passed a law reserving thirty percent of government positions for women; and in 2024 it banned child marriage, setting the legal age at eighteen. There's still unfinished business — female genital cutting is still legal and very common, and activists are pushing to ban that next — but these are real, internationally praised steps forward.
Politics & Economy · Resources

From “blood diamonds” to transparency

The 969-carat “Star of Sierra Leone” (1972) · Z Jason, CC BY-SA · Wikimedia Commons
  • Mineral-rich: diamonds, rutile (a top global titanium-ore source), bauxite, iron ore, and gold — minerals are 70%+ of exports.
  • The war’s blood diamonds spurred the global Kimberley Process (2003), which certifies rough diamonds as conflict-free.
  • Sierra Leone joined the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative, publishing what miners pay and government receives (compliant since 2014).
  • New laws — the Extractive Industries Revenue Act (2018) and Mines & Minerals Development Act (2022) — tightened oversight.

Legal diamond exports rebounded sharply, though smuggling and artisanal-mining poverty persist.

Let me come back to those diamonds, because there's a real reform story here. Sierra Leone is rich in minerals — not just diamonds, but rutile, which is a titanium ore it's one of the world's biggest producers of, plus bauxite, iron ore, and gold. Minerals are more than seventy percent of everything the country exports. The problem, as we saw, was "blood diamonds" funding the war. That scandal pushed the whole world to act: in 2003 they created the Kimberley Process, an international system that certifies rough diamonds as conflict-free, so buyers know their diamond didn't pay for a war. Sierra Leone went further and joined the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative — basically a promise to publish exactly what mining companies pay and what the government takes in, so the money can't just disappear. It's been officially compliant since 2014. And it passed new laws — a revenue act in 2018 and a big mining law in 2022 — to tighten control over the sector. The result: legal diamond exports bounced back enormously after the war. It's not perfect — smuggling and poverty among small-scale miners are still real problems — but it's a genuine effort to turn a curse into something that actually funds the country.
Current Events · 2024–2026

Today’s hardest problems

  • Kush drug crisis: President Bio declared a national emergency in 2024; the drug contains synthetic opioids (nitazenes) deadlier than fentanyl.
  • Mpox: Sierra Leone became the epicenter of Africa’s 2025 outbreak — 5,000+ cases.
  • Among the world’s poorest (ranked ~184 of 193); minerals are 70%+ of exports.
  • A huge young population with too few jobs drives much of the instability.
Mining, a mainstay of the economy · Wikimedia Commons
So what are the biggest problems right now, in 2024 to 2026? Three stand out. First, drugs. A cheap street drug called "kush" has become so devastating among young people that President Bio declared a national emergency over it in 2024. Testing showed it contains synthetic opioids even stronger than fentanyl, so it's killing users who have no idea how dangerous it is. Second, disease. In 2025 Sierra Leone became the center of Africa's mpox outbreak, with over five thousand cases — again straining that fragile health system. And third, poverty. It's one of the poorest countries on earth, ranked around 184th out of 193, and it depends heavily on selling raw minerals like diamonds. Underneath all of this is that huge young population with not enough jobs — which fuels the drugs and the political unrest.
Culture · Tourism

Beaches, rainforest, and wildlife

River No. 2 Beach, Freetown Peninsula · Wikimedia Commons
  • The Freetown Peninsula has white-sand beaches like River No. 2 and Tokeh, plus the surf beach at Bureh.
  • Tiwai Island, a rainforest reserve, has one of the world’s richest mixes of primates and the rare pygmy hippopotamus.
  • More wild places: the Banana Islands, Outamba-Kilimi National Park, and the Tacugama chimps.
  • Tourism is small but growing (~117,000 visitors in 2024) after the setbacks of war and Ebola.
Sierra Leone is also, quite simply, beautiful — and it's trying to build a tourism industry around that. Right outside Freetown, the peninsula is lined with stunning white-sand beaches like River No. 2 and Tokeh, and a surf beach at Bureh. For nature, the highlight is Tiwai Island, a rainforest island with one of the highest concentrations of monkey species anywhere on Earth, and it's a refuge for the rare pygmy hippopotamus — a small, elusive relative of the regular hippo. There's also the Banana Islands, a national park up north called Outamba-Kilimi, and of course the Tacugama chimpanzee sanctuary. Tourism was hammered by the civil war and then the Ebola outbreak, so it's still small — around a hundred and seventeen thousand visitors in 2024 — but it's growing, and the government is betting it could become a real part of the economy.
Conclusion

Reasons for hope

Western chimpanzee — national animal · Wikimedia Commons
  • Free Quality Education made school free and pushed enrollment up to roughly 2.5 million students.
  • Its famous Tacugama chimp sanctuary closed in protest over illegal logging in 2025 — and won, reopening months later.
  • A booming music scene, a national football team, and rare interfaith harmony.

Takeaway: a young democracy, still finding its footing — scarred by its past, but reforming and hopeful.

I want to end on hope, because Sierra Leone's story isn't only hardship. The government's "Free Quality Education" program made school free and pushed enrollment up to around two and a half million kids. Here's my favorite story: the country's famous chimpanzee sanctuary was so angry about illegal logging that in 2025 it shut its gates in protest — and it actually worked; the government backed down and it reopened. Even the chimps won a political fight. And there's a thriving music scene, a national football team, and that rare, genuine harmony between Muslims and Christians. So my one takeaway is this: Sierra Leone is a young democracy still finding its footing — scarred by its past, but reforming, and hopeful about its future.
