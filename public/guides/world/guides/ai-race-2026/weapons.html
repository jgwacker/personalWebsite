Section 5

Autonomous Weapons and the Military AI Question

The first four sections traced civilian AI as if its strategic implications could be bracketed. They cannot. The same supply chains and the same race dynamics now run through every major military, and the diplomatic machinery built to constrain them is visibly failing. Autonomous weapons are where the cooperation problem of Section 2 is most acute, where the leverage of Section 3 is most contested, and where the stakes for civilian life are highest.

Defining the category

Key term — Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS)

The UN Group of Governmental Experts adopted a working definition in March 2026: a weapons system that “can identify, select, and engage a target, without intervention by a human operator in the execution of these tasks.”UN GGE The definition deliberately excludes remotely piloted drones, even those with autonomous flight control, on the grounds that a human still makes the engagement decision. It includes loitering munitions and autonomous turrets that select and engage targets on their own.

The category is not hypothetical. The Israeli Harpy has loitered over designated areas and attacked enemy emitters since the 1990s. A 2021 UN Panel of Experts report alleged that the Turkish Kargu-2 engaged targets autonomously in Libya in 2020; the manufacturer disputes the claim, and independent investigators have not fully verified it. The Russian Lancet-3 has been the most-cited live deployment of the Ukraine war and reportedly carries autonomous target-recognition modes. The Israeli campaign in Gaza has drawn extensive reporting on AI-targeting systems known as Lavender and Gospel, first described by +972 Magazine on the basis of anonymous IDF sources; the IDF disputes the characterization. Human-rights investigators and legal scholars remain divided on what has been empirically established.

Three parallel diplomatic tracks

The international debate runs on three tracks. None has produced a binding treaty.

UN CCW GGE on LAWS

The Group of Governmental Experts has operated under the Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons since 2014. The CCW decides by consensus, which gives any single member state a veto over progress. The United States, Russia, India, and Israel routinely exercise it. The final CCW Review Conference is scheduled for November 2026.

REAIM Summit series

Responsible AI in the Military Domain: The Hague (February 2023), Seoul (September 2024), A Coruña (February 2026). The 2026 outcome statement was signed by only 35 of 85 attending states — the first time both the United States and China declined to sign.

U.S. Political Declaration

Launched at REAIM 2023 and signed by roughly 60 states by 2026. The declaration articulates principles for responsible military AI use and is explicitly designed as a non-binding alternative to a treaty. Washington presents it as the appropriate format; pro-treaty states read it as a delaying tactic.

Bilateral and minilateral

U.S.–China DoD-to-DoD military AI dialogue (sporadic, restarted 2023, suspended after Taiwan visits); NATO’s AI strategy; AUKUS Pillar 2 on advanced capabilities; Five Eyes coordination on AI in intelligence.

The November 2025 UNGA vote

On November 6, 2025, the First Committee of the UN General Assembly passed a resolution on autonomous weapons by 164 in favor, 6 against, and 7 abstentions.campaign The six no votes were Belarus, Burundi, North Korea, Israel, Russia, and the United States. The seven abstentions included China and Iran. The resolution asked the Secretary-General to convene a structured consultation process and report by 2026. It is not legally binding, but it is the clearest political signal to date that the global majority wants a binding instrument and that none of the major military powers will agree to one.

Why no treaty has stuck

Three structural reasons explain the deadlock.

  1. The CCW consensus rule. The forum where weapons-treaty work has historically happened operates by consensus. With the United States, Russia, India, and Israel reliably blocking, the GGE has spent more than a decade producing no binding decision. More than 70 states want to negotiate; the institutional rules prevent it.
  2. The first-mover dynamic. Every major military power calculates that the cost of being second to a battlefield-effective autonomous system is higher than the cost of being first. This is the textbook security dilemma: one state’s defensive investments appear offensive to a rival, who responds in kind, producing an equilibrium more militarized than any single state would have chosen.
  3. The dual-use interface. Civilian frontier models can in principle help select targets, write malicious code, or assist operational planning. The international institutions built for cleanly categorized weapons — chemical, biological, anti-personnel landmines — struggle to apply to a substrate technology embedded across every part of a modern military’s information system.

The Anthropic–Pentagon episode

Between February and May 2026 the civilian and military tracks collided in the most visible incident to date. The Pentagon pressed Anthropic to remove safety constraints from its models for autonomous-weapons applications and threatened to terminate a $200 million contract if Anthropic refused. Anthropic refused. The Trump administration formally designated the company a “supply chain risk” and ordered federal agencies to cease business with it. A federal judge enjoined the blacklist in March 2026. By May, the Pentagon had signed classified AI agreements with eight other firms.CNN

The episode condensed every theme of the section into a single dispute. Civilian safety practice, voluntary corporate commitments, military procurement, and constitutional law collided. Anthropic sent a costly signal and paid for it with a major contract and a federal blacklist. The Pentagon substituted suppliers. Neither side moved. The international diplomatic machinery had nothing to say.

What realist theory predicts

The pattern fits the theory closely. When a strategic technology emerges, the major military powers resist binding constraints on its development while the rest of the international community pushes for norms it cannot enforce. The closest historical parallel is not the Chemical Weapons Convention, which worked because the major powers cooperated, but the Ottawa Process on landmines, which advanced through majority-vote politics around great-power vetoes. The deeper analogue is nuclear: the powers settled into a permanent legal inequality (the NPT) rather than a ban, and they did so because each calculated it could not afford the alternative. Autonomous weapons appear to be heading the same way. The institutionalist counter — that voluntary frameworks, summits, and UNGA votes accumulate into the political ground on which a binding instrument might eventually rest — is the argument International Cooperation on AI takes up in detail; on the record visible from 2026, the realist reading describes what has actually happened.
