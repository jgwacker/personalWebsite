Section 4

The Compute Buildout Race

If the chip war was about denying compute to a rival, the buildout race is about producing as much of it as possible. Three blocs are now sprinting in parallel: the United States, behind a private-sector capital wave and federal incentives; the Gulf, converting sovereign wealth into an independent infrastructure pole; and China, assembling a closed-loop domestic stack. The pattern has the structural features of an arms race: tightly coupled investment cycles, security-framed justifications, and the assumption that being second is the dangerous outcome.

The American sprint — Stargate and the hyperscalers

On January 21, 2025, the first full day of the new administration, President Trump appeared with Sam Altman, Larry Ellison, and Masayoshi Son to announce Stargate, a $500-billion four-year AI-infrastructure venture funded by OpenAI, Oracle, and SoftBank.OpenAI Delivery to date:

Sep 2025

The first Stargate data center comes online in Abilene, Texas, on Oracle infrastructure with Nvidia chips.

Sep 2025

Five additional sites are announced — Shackelford County (TX), Doña Ana County (NM), Lordstown (OH), Milam County (TX), and an undisclosed Midwestern location — for a combined planned capacity of roughly 7 GW.

2025 total

Roughly 90 percent of every data-center power project on the U.S. calendar (about 50 GW) was announced during 2025 alone.

Stargate sits inside a broader hyperscaler wave. Amazon, Microsoft, Google, and Meta together spent $416 billion on infrastructure in 2025 and are projected to spend approximately $645 billion in 2026.data The CHIPS Act has appropriated $52.7 billion in domestic semiconductor incentives. Intel’s Arizona Fab 52 entered 2nm-class production in October 2025 — the first U.S. facility ever to cross that threshold. The federal government and the corporate sector are now coordinated in treating the AI buildout as a strategic priority on the scale of the Apollo program.

The Gulf enters as a third pole

The most consequential strategic-energy development of 2025–26 is the arrival of the Gulf states as AI-infrastructure players. Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates have together committed more than $130 billion to AI infrastructure as part of a long-running diversification away from hydrocarbon revenue.OilPrice The signature move came in May 2025, when the UAE’s G42 announced a 5 GW AI campus in Abu Dhabi with OpenAI, Nvidia, Oracle, Cisco, and SoftBank — the largest piece of AI infrastructure ever planned outside the United States. Stargate UAE, as it has become known, draws on the Barakah nuclear plant, the Al Dhafra solar complex, and natural gas.

Why the Gulf matters strategically

The Gulf states are pursuing three goals simultaneously: hedging against the long-term decline of oil revenue; purchasing a seat at the AI policy table that pure compute consumption could not buy them; and building an alternative center of frontier-AI infrastructure that is neither Silicon Valley nor China. A Riyadh-Washington or Abu Dhabi-Beijing axis on AI hardware is a structural option that did not exist in 2022. In realist terms, multipolarity has arrived earlier than the analysts expected.

China’s domestic stack

The chip war made China’s indigenous-substitute program a national priority. The result is the slow construction of the Huawei–SMIC stack: SMIC manufacturing 7nm chips using DUV multi-patterning at 20–40 percent yields, and Huawei designing the Ascend 910B and 910C accelerators. The 910C delivers roughly 60 percent of Nvidia H100 performance for inference workloads. Domestic Chinese memory makers fill in the surrounding components. Big Fund III, the country’s third National Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund, raised approximately $47 billion in 2024 alone.MERICS

The program is the closest thing in modern industrial history to a from-scratch national chip stack. It cannot match the leading edge in 2026. CSIS analysts judge it has made “strategically significant progress” toward a closed-loop alternative — an option that, if it matures, reduces Chinese dependence on the Taiwan–Netherlands–Korea–Japan supply chain and reshapes the leverage map.

Electricity is the new binding constraint

The hard problem is no longer silicon; it is electricity. The International Energy Agency’s 2025 Energy and AI report projects global data-center demand to roughly double from 415 TWh in 2024 to about 950 TWh by 2030, around 3 percent of world electricity production.IEA In Virginia alone, data centers are projected to consume between 41 and 59 percent of state electricity by 2030. The hyperscalers have responded by underwriting a near-complete remake of American baseload generation.

Restarted nuclear

Microsoft signed a 20-year contract for 835 MW from the restarted Three Mile Island — the first U.S. plant ever restarted for a single customer. Georgia’s Vogtle 3 and 4 came online in 2024 after $35 billion and 15 years of construction.

Small modular reactors

Microsoft, Google, and Amazon have signed SMR deals with Kairos Power, X-energy, and others. First deployments are expected between 2029 and 2033, leaving natural gas as the bridge fuel.

Gulf energy

Saudi Arabia is planning its first nuclear plant. The UAE’s Barakah supplies roughly 25 percent of national electricity and powers Stargate UAE. AI demand is reshaping Gulf energy policy as much as Gulf capital is reshaping AI.

Geothermal

Google-backed Fervo is moving enhanced geothermal from the research frontier into mainstream planning. AI demand has become the catalyst the technology had been waiting for.

Reading the race

Set the four blocs side by side and the strategy becomes visible.

  • The United States is converting its private-sector lead into infrastructure scale, with the federal government supplying CHIPS Act incentives and political cover.
  • China is building a closed-loop alternative that can survive a hostile supply chain, accepting lower efficiency in exchange for higher sovereignty.
  • The Gulf is converting sovereign-wealth capital into an independent third pole, betting that AI hardware is the strategic asset of the post-oil era.
  • The European Union is moving slower and smaller, attempting to preserve enough domestic capacity that the Brussels effect remains credible without a competitive domestic AI sector.

The chip war asked who controls the chokepoints. The buildout race asks who builds the next decade of compute.
