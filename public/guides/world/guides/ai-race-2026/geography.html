Section 2

The Geography of Dependence

If compute is the chokepoint, the chokepoint has an address. Six firms in five countries hold the binding constraints on every frontier model, and a Dutch export decision can move share prices in Tokyo, Seoul, and Beijing in the same trading session. The map of those dependencies is the geography of leverage in the AI competition.

The realist tradition asks a simple question of any strategic technology: who depends on whom, and at what cost can the dependence be broken? Applied to the AI supply chain, the answer narrows quickly. A small number of countries — and inside them a smaller number of firms — control every step that cannot be moved. Their commercial decisions now function as foreign policy, and their foreign-policy decisions move markets.

Six chokepoints, one supply chain

Every frontier model passes through the same set of bottlenecks. Each one resolves to a single country and, in two cases, a single firm.

The 2025 bottleneck moved from silicon to packaging

The “chip shortage” of 2024 was misread. The binding constraint through 2025 was advanced packaging — specifically TSMC’s CoWoS (chip-on-wafer-on-substrate) process, which mounts the GPU die and the HBM stacks together on a single substrate. CoWoS capacity is fully booked through 2026–27, with Nvidia alone reserving more than half. The shortage migrated from the chips themselves to the substrate that joins them.Epoch AI

What dependence costs

Each dependency carries its own switching cost. Three of the six cannot be broken within a decade on any realistic plan.

  • ASML. No second supplier exists. The United States and Japan began serious research on EUV alternatives in 2023 and 2024; first prototypes are unlikely before the 2030s.
  • TSMC at 3nm and below. Samsung is the only firm in the leading-edge league, and its position has weakened: yields collapsed in 2024–25 and its foundry market share fell to 7.7 percent in Q1 2025. The Arizona expansion brings some leading-edge production to U.S. soil, but TSMC itself expects Arizona to remain a generation behind Taiwan into the 2030s.
  • Japan’s EUV photoresists. Shin-Etsu, JSR, and TOK hold near-total share. China’s domestic chemistry is roughly a decade behind.

The other three dependencies — Korean HBM, U.S. fabs, China’s SMIC alternative — are contested rather than locked. They are where the policy fights of the next five years will be decided.

What the map teaches

Key term — Strategic dependence

In realist theory, a state is strategically dependent on another when it cannot substitute away from inputs the other controls within a politically feasible timeframe. The supplier acquires coercive leverage — the implicit capacity to disrupt the dependent state’s economic or military operations by withholding the input. Dependence is rarely symmetric: a state often relies on inputs from a partner that in turn relies on its market for those inputs. Both sides hold leverage, and both face costs from breaking the relationship. This is the structural shape of the U.S.–Taiwan, U.S.–Netherlands, and U.S.–China relationships in 2026.CSIS

The supply chain reads as a set of mutual hostages.

  • The United States depends on Taiwan for leading-edge chips; Taiwan depends on the United States for security.
  • The United States depends on the Netherlands for EUV machines; the Netherlands depends on the United States for the alliance architecture that secures its export market.
  • China depends on the global supply chain for advanced chips; the global supply chain depends on China as a buyer of mature-node output and a source of critical minerals.

None of these dependencies can be severed without enormous mutual cost. That is why the United States reached for unilateral controls in 2022 — and why those controls produced strategic adaptation rather than capitulation. Section 3 takes up the attempt and its consequences.

What to carry into Section 3

The map shows where leverage sits. The next question is what happens when a state tries to use it. The chip war is the first and most studied attempt to convert this geography into strategic effect.
