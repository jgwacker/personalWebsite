Section 3

The Chip War

In October 2022 the United States used its position in the supply chain to slow a peer competitor’s AI program. Four years on, the chip export controls are the decade’s clearest case of techno-economic statecraft against a great power. They have produced costly Chinese adaptation rather than capitulation — and a textbook illustration of the limits of unilateral coercion in a deeply interdependent economy.

This section walks the policy chronologically, then asks the realist questions: what did the United States spend, what did it gain, and how did the target adapt. The conclusion is conceptual. Even a partially successful unilateral action does not end a strategic competition; it reshapes the terrain on which the next round will be fought.

The timeline

7 October 2022

The Bureau of Industry and Security publishes sweeping export controls on advanced chips and the equipment that produces them. The rule targets China and Macau alone.CSET

2022–2023

The original rule contains a loophole. Nvidia sells slightly downgraded A800 and H800 chips designed to fall below the threshold; it books more than $9 billion in Chinese revenue before the rule is tightened.

17 October 2023

The updated rule closes the workaround, introduces a three-part test for what qualifies as an AI chip, and extends controls to 44 additional countries to block re-export through third parties.

2023–2024

The Netherlands restricts ASML’s DUV machines. Japan adds 23 categories of semiconductor equipment to its export-control list in June 2023 and 21 more in April 2024. The chokepoint alliance holds.

20 January 2025

DeepSeek releases R1, matching U.S. frontier models on benchmarks. The training run relied largely on H800s purchased before the rule tightened. Nvidia’s stock falls 17 percent in a single day.CSIS

2025–2026

The Trump administration tightens controls on Huawei’s Ascend stack and on chip-on-chip packaging exports, while loosening enforcement in selected allied jurisdictions for political reasons. The regime stays in place; its strategic logic begins to age.

The leakage problem

Unilateral export controls share a structural weakness: they require every firm in the supply chain to comply, and the controlled good is too valuable for compliance to be uniform. The chips moved.

The algorithms moved too. DeepSeek’s V3 and R1 were engineered around the constraints the controls imposed: limited inter-chip bandwidth, smaller cluster sizes, fewer training tokens per parameter. The mixture-of-experts architecture, sparse attention, and the “lightning indexer” all read as responses to a world in which compute is scarce. The export controls did not stop DeepSeek; they shaped the architecture DeepSeek chose.

Did it work?

The controls slowed China’s frontier at the leading edge and bought American laboratories a capability lead measured in years. SMIC’s 7nm yields remain poor; without EUV the 3nm threshold is effectively closed to Chinese fabs; DeepSeek matched 2024-era U.S. models on benchmarks but has not leapfrogged them.CSIS That is the case for the policy.

The counter is that the same controls accelerated China’s domestic chip-self-sufficiency program — a program that without external pressure might have remained politically underfunded — while costing U.S. firms revenue and research-and-development capacity. If algorithmic efficiency continues to improve, the leverage value of compute controls falls with it.limits The counter is real, but it argues against the durability of the leverage, not against the fact that the leverage has so far been exercised to effect.

What realist theory predicted

Key term — Strategic adaptation

Realist theory expects a target of economic coercion to respond in three overlapping stages: protest the action diplomatically, substitute around it where possible, and develop indigenous alternatives over time. The chip war produced all three from China — formal WTO complaints (rejected as legally weak), aggressive smuggling and shell-company sourcing, and the Big Fund III ($47 billion) underwriting a Huawei–SMIC stack as the long-run substitute. The pattern itself is unsurprising. What is surprising is how rapidly the substitution and indigenous stages overlapped.

The deeper lesson

The chip war is the cleanest available case study of three propositions in realist theory.

  1. Chokepoint control is a real source of coercive leverage, but only as long as the alliance holds and the controlled good remains essential.
  2. Unilateral action against a peer competitor produces adaptation, not capitulation. The relevant questions are what the adaptation looks like and how fast it scales.
  3. The strategic timeline matters more than the snapshot. A control regime that buys the controller five years is a partial victory. One that triggers a crash indigenous-substitute program may, in retrospect, look like an own goal.

The chip war slowed China’s frontier at a real cost to U.S. firms, and it produced the alignment of Tokyo, Seoul, and The Hague that the policy needed to have teeth. Whether the trade was worth it is a judgment, not a calculation. Section 4 turns to what the United States, the Gulf, and China have built since the chip war became the new normal.
