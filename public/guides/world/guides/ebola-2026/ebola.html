Section 1

What Ebola Is

Before the politics, the biology. Ebola is rare, frightening, and — caught early — beatable. Knowing how it works explains almost everything about the global response.

Ebola is a virus that causes a severe, often deadly illness in people. Scientists first met it in 1976 near the Ebola River in what is now the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and named it after the river because they couldn’t yet say which village it came from.history Under a microscope the virus looks like a long, looping thread — it belongs to a family aptly nicknamed the “thread viruses.”

Not one virus, but four

“Ebola” is really a small family of related viruses. Four species cause disease in humans, and they are not equally dangerous. The figures below are historical case-fatality rates — the share of diagnosed patients who died — which fall sharply when good supportive care is available.WHO

The strain spreading now: Bundibugyo

The 2026 outbreak is caused by the Bundibugyo virus, named for the district in western Uganda where it first appeared in 2007. It is genuinely rare: before this year there had been only two known outbreaks, in Uganda (2007) and Congo (2012).Gavi

The fact that shapes everything

There is no licensed vaccine and no approved treatment for Bundibugyo virus. The famous Ebola vaccine, ERVEBO, and the two approved antibody drugs all target a different species — Zaire. Bundibugyo is more than 30% genetically distinct, different enough that defenses built against Zaire barely recognize it.Sci Am Doctors are left with supportive care — fluids and treating complications — and the only real weapons are the old ones: find cases fast, isolate them, and bury the dead safely.

How it spreads — and how it doesn’t

Ebola usually enters humans through a spillover: someone touches an infected wild animal. Fruit bats are the suspected natural host, carrying the virus without getting sick.WHO After that first jump, it passes person to person — but only in specific ways.

How it spreads

  • Direct contact with an infected person’s blood or bodily fluids
  • Touching a body after death — the most infectious moment of all
  • Contact through broken skin or the eyes, nose, and mouth

How it does not spread

  • Through the air, the way flu or COVID-19 can
  • By casual contact — sharing a bus, a room, a sidewalk
  • From a person who has no symptoms yet

Because it isn’t airborne, Ebola is actually harder to catch than many common viruses. That single biological fact is why the CDC calls the risk to the general public low, and why the response focuses on the small circle of people in close contact with the sick.

The 21-day clock

After infection, a person feels fine for anywhere from 2 to 21 days — and during that time they are not contagious.WHO Then symptoms hit: sudden fever, aches, and exhaustion that look just like malaria, followed in severe cases by vomiting, diarrhea, and sometimes bleeding. Death, when it comes, is usually 6 to 16 days after symptoms start.

Why the response looks the way it does

That 21-day window is the reason for two rituals of every Ebola response. Contact tracing: track down everyone a patient met and watch them for 21 days, isolating them the instant they fall ill. Safe burials: a corpse carries the highest viral load of all, and traditional funerals that involve washing and touching the body drove more than half of all cases in the 2014–16 epidemic.study Do both well and the chains of transmission break.

One outbreak, one lesson from history

The very first Ebola outbreak, at Yambuku in 1976, killed 280 of 318 people — and a hospital made it worse, reusing unsterilized needles that spread the virus to dozens of patients.source The lesson echoes in 2026: when health systems are weak or overwhelmed, the places meant to heal can amplify the disease instead. That’s the bridge from biology to politics — and to the rest of this site.
