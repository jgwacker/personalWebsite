Glossary

Bundibugyo virus
One of four Ebola species that infect people, first identified in 2007 in Uganda. It causes the 2026 outbreak and has no licensed vaccine or treatment.
Case-fatality rate (CFR)
The share of diagnosed patients who die from a disease. For Ebola it ranges roughly 25–90% depending on the species and the quality of care.
Zoonotic spillover
When a disease jumps from animals to humans. Ebola’s natural host is thought to be fruit bats.
ERVEBO
The licensed Ebola vaccine (approved 2019). It protects only against the Zaire species, not Bundibugyo.
Contact tracing
Finding everyone an infected person met and monitoring them (for Ebola, 21 days) so they can be isolated the moment they fall ill.
PHEIC
Public Health Emergency of International Concern — the WHO’s highest alert level, declared by its Director-General. Declared for this outbreak on 17 May 2026.
IHR (International Health Regulations, 2005)
A legally binding agreement among 196 countries requiring them to report outbreaks and build core detection-and-response capacities.
WHO & the World Health Assembly
The World Health Organization is the UN’s health agency; the World Health Assembly is its decision-making body, where each member country gets one vote.
Africa CDC
The African Union’s disease-control agency (launched 2017), able since 2022 to declare its own continental health emergencies.
GHSA (Global Health Security Agenda)
A 40+ country partnership launched in 2014, with U.S. leadership, to help countries build outbreak-response capacity.
Gavi
The Vaccine Alliance — finances and stockpiles vaccines for lower-income countries.
MSF (Doctors Without Borders)
An independent medical charity that runs treatment in war zones and outbreaks, often before governments arrive.
USAID
The U.S. Agency for International Development (1961–2025); once the world’s largest aid agency, shut down and folded into the State Department in 2025.
PEPFAR
The U.S. President’s Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (2003), credited with saving an estimated 26 million lives.
DART (Disaster Assistance Response Team)
USAID’s rapid-deployment emergency team, able to reach a disaster within 24–48 hours.
America First Global Health Strategy / MOU
The 2025 U.S. policy replacing multilateral aid with bilateral, five-year Memoranda of Understanding (MOUs) negotiated country by country.
Sovereignty vs. the global commons
Sovereignty is a country’s right to govern itself; the global commons are shared resources (like freedom from pandemics) that no one owns but all depend on.
Free-rider problem
When an actor enjoys a shared benefit without helping pay for it — e.g., a country that underfunds surveillance but relies on its neighbors’.
Multilateralism vs. bilateralism
Multilateralism is many countries acting through shared institutions; bilateralism is two countries dealing directly.
Global health security
The idea that an outbreak anywhere is a security threat everywhere, deserving the seriousness of a military danger.
Soft power
Influence gained through attraction and goodwill rather than force (a term coined by Joseph Nye).
Humanitarian intervention
Outsiders stepping in to protect people another government can’t or won’t — sometimes before a formal invitation.
M23 (“March 23 Movement”)
A Rwanda-backed armed group controlling parts of eastern Congo, including Goma — a major obstacle to the 2026 Ebola response.
