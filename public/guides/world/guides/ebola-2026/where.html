Where the Outbreak Is Happening

The 2026 outbreak is centered in eastern Congo and has crossed into Uganda. But its deadliest feature isn’t on any map of cases — it’s a war.

How it unfolded

25 April 2026

The first known patient falls ill in Ituri Province, in Congo’s rainforested northeast — most likely from an animal spillover.

14–15 May

Lab tests confirm Bundibugyo virus. Congo declares its 17th Ebola outbreak since 1976.

15–16 May

Two unconnected cases turn up in Kampala, Uganda’s capital — both travelers from Congo. The outbreak is now international.

17 May

The WHO declares a Public Health Emergency of International Concern, its highest alarm. A single suspected case in Goma is reported (it would later test negative on re-evaluation).

18 May

The virus spreads to the trading hub of Butembo. The U.S. announces a 21-day entry prohibition for certain non-U.S. nationals from DR Congo, Uganda, and South Sudan, reroutes affected travelers to four airports (IAD, ATL, IAH, JFK), and adds enhanced screening.STAT

21 May

A case is confirmed in South Kivu — the patient had traveled from Tshopo Province (Kisangani). Contact-tracing follow-up is climbing as the response scales.

~26 May

Confirmed and suspected cases together pass 1,000; in the days that follow, the suspected pool shrinks sharply as testing catches up and many cases are reclassified or excluded.

28 May 2026

The WHO recommends against off-label use of ERVEBO for this Bundibugyo outbreak, noting it protects only against the Zaire species.WHO

30 May 2026

An ADF (Allied Democratic Forces) massacre in Beni adds a second armed-group dimension to the response; three confirmed patients flee treatment centers in the chaos.

1 June 2026

CEPI announces $62 million to fast-track three Bundibugyo vaccine candidates: Moderna mRNA ($50M), Oxford/Serum Institute ($8.6M), and IAVI ($3.2M).CEPI

3 June 2026

Director-General Tedros holds a media briefing and calls for a humanitarian ceasefire to enable the response.WHO

4 June 2026

A burial team is attacked in Katana, South Kivu (an M23-controlled area); 11 patients flee isolation facilities in Ituri.

5 June 2026

The State Department announces an additional $38 million, bringing cumulative direct U.S. Ebola-response funding above $200 million.State Dept

The real obstacle: a war on top of an outbreak

Eastern Congo is one of the world’s longest-running conflict zones. Goma — a city of around two million and the gateway for aid into the region — is held by M23, a Rwanda-backed rebel group that now runs a parallel government across much of North and South Kivu.background Fighting an invisible virus is hard enough; doing it in a war zone may be impossible with the usual tools.

“We cannot build community trust or isolate the sick while bombs are falling.” — Dr. Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, WHO Director-General

Look back at Section 1: the entire Ebola playbook is contact tracing and safe burials. Conflict breaks both.

  • Tracing is climbing but incomplete. Health workers must visit every contact daily for 21 days. Of roughly 2,635 contacts in Ituri and North Kivu, about 45% are now being followed, alongside 436 contacts in Uganda — up sharply from a fifth in late May, but still well short of what the playbook demands.WHO
  • Treatment centers are attacked. Residents stormed a health center in Rwampara demanding the bodies of relatives, and a Doctors Without Borders tent in Mongbwalu was set on fire after a patient died.Al Jazeera
  • People flee — and carry the virus. Military operations push hundreds of thousands to move, spreading infection across health zones and over borders.
  • Hunger weakens everyone. Nearly 10 million people in the three affected provinces face acute hunger, and malnourished bodies fight infection poorly.UN News
Why this is an international-relations story

This is the same region where the 2018–19 Ebola outbreak — fought amid the same violence — dragged on for 22 months and killed 2,299 people, the second-worst in history.WHO Stopping Ebola here isn’t only a medical job; it needs security, diplomacy with armed groups, and outside help. That is why the next two sections turn to the global system built to provide that help — and to what happens when a major power steps away from it.
