Looking Back: the 2014 Ebola Crisis

A little over a decade ago, the United States threw everything at an Ebola epidemic — troops, money, and a vaccine effort. Comparing then with now is the clearest way to see what’s changed.

In late 2013, Ebola began spreading in West Africa, and through 2014–2016 it became the largest Ebola epidemic in history. It struck three countries — Guinea, Liberia, and Sierra Leone — that had weak health systems and porous borders.

How the United States responded

The 2014 response was unusually large — and it leaned on the same institutions Section 5 just discussed.

The military: Operation United Assistance

President Obama ordered up to ~3,000 troops to Liberia. They didn’t fight the virus directly — they built Ebola Treatment Units and trained health workers, things the military could do at scale and speed.source

The civilians: USAID’s DART

A 28-person Disaster Assistance Response Team led the U.S. effort on the ground, airlifting 130,000 sets of protective equipment and committing over $100 million within the first weeks.White House

The coordinator: an “Ebola czar”

Obama appointed Ron Klain to run the whole interagency response and calm a frightened public — a single point of accountability.PBS

The crisis came home

Ebola reached the United States, too. In September 2014, a Liberian man named Thomas Eric Duncan was diagnosed in Dallas — the first case identified on U.S. soil. He died on October 8. Two nurses who treated him, Nina Pham and Amber Vinson, caught the virus and survived.CDC The country was frightened, and a political fight broke out.

A debate that still repeats — the travel ban

Many in Congress demanded a ban on flights from West Africa. Obama and public-health experts opposed it. Their reasoning, backed by experience with Ebola, H1N1, and later COVID-19: broad travel bans delay but don’t prevent a virus’s arrival, block the doctors and supplies that need to move toward the outbreak, and push travelers onto hidden routes that are harder to track. Compare this with the airport-screening approach used in 2026 (Section 3) — different tools, the same underlying dilemma.

The vaccine that came out of it — and why it can’t help now

One lasting win came from this era. A vaccine, ERVEBO, was tested during the West Africa epidemic, used widely in a later 2018–2020 Congo outbreak, and won FDA approval in 2019.CDC But — as Section 1 explained — ERVEBO protects only against the Zaire species. The 2026 outbreak is Bundibugyo, a different species with no approved vaccine. The tool exists; it just doesn’t fit this lock.

The nugget

History doesn’t tell you what’s right — but it gives you a baseline. In 2014 the United States treated a distant outbreak as its own emergency. In 2026 it has chosen a very different role. Whether that’s prudence or retreat is exactly the question Section 5 asked you to weigh. Now you’ve seen both the argument and the precedent.
