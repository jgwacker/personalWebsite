Section 4

The Global Health System

When an outbreak is declared, a whole machine switches on — agencies, rules, and treaties built over decades. Here’s who does what, and how the pieces fit.

There is no world government for disease. Instead there is a patchwork of organizations and agreements that only work when countries choose to use them. Four parts matter most: the WHO, the rulebook called the IHR, the alarm called a PHEIC, and a supporting cast of regional bodies and funders.

The WHO: convener, not commander

The World Health Organization, founded in 1948, is the United Nations’ health agency. It sets standards, publishes guidance, sends technical teams, and runs the global outbreak-alert system. What it cannot do is give orders: it has influence, not authority over sovereign states.

Its decisions are made by member countries through the World Health Assembly — a kind of parliament where all 194 members get one vote — which elects a Director-General to lead between meetings. The catch is money. Only about 20% of the WHO’s budget comes from the predictable dues countries owe; the other 80% is voluntary, and roughly 87% of that is earmarked by donors for specific projects.WHO That leaves the agency with surprisingly little money it can move freely to wherever an emergency erupts.

A system under strain

The United States was historically the WHO’s largest single funder, about 15.6% of its revenue. After the U.S. withdrawal was completed in January 2026, the WHO cut roughly 3,000 jobs and now faces a multi-billion-dollar gap; the Gates Foundation became its biggest contributor.KFF We unpack that decision and the debate around it in Section 5.

The IHR: the rulebook

Key term — IHR (2005)

The International Health Regulations are a legally binding agreement among 196 countries. They require each country to (1) report dangerous outbreaks to the WHO quickly, and (2) build “core capacities” — labs, surveillance, trained staff, border health checks. They were rewritten after the 2003 SARS crisis showed the old rules couldn’t handle a fast new threat.CDC

The IHR’s weakness is its lack of teeth. The WHO can’t fine a country that hides an outbreak or one that imposes a pointless travel ban. The whole system runs on reputation, diplomatic pressure, and each country’s own interest in a world that actually reports disease.source

The PHEIC: the highest alarm

A Public Health Emergency of International Concern is the WHO’s top alert level — reserved for an “extraordinary event” that threatens other countries and needs a coordinated response. Only the Director-General can declare one, usually after consulting an expert Emergency Committee. Before 2026 it had been used just seven times in history, for crises like H1N1 flu, Zika, and COVID-19.WHO

On 17 May 2026, the Director-General declared this Ebola outbreak a PHEIC — and did so before the Emergency Committee had formally met, a procedural first that signaled real urgency. A declaration sorts the world into risk tiers, each with its own to-do list:

  • Affected countries (Congo, Uganda): declare a national emergency, trace every contact for 21 days, run safe burials, report cases daily.
  • Neighbors: tighten border surveillance, set up testing labs, pre-position protective gear.
  • Everyone else: watch for feverish travelers and share accurate information.

A PHEIC is also a flare sent to donors, charities, and drug companies: resources and attention are needed here, now.

The supporting cast

The WHO sits at the center, but it never works alone. These are the other players that show up in an Ebola response:

Africa CDC

The African Union’s own disease agency, launched in 2017 after the 2014 Ebola disaster. Since 2022 it can declare its own continental emergencies — a push for African “health sovereignty.”

GHSA

The Global Health Security Agenda, a 40+ country partnership launched in 2014 (with U.S. leadership) to help countries actually build the capacities the IHR requires.

Gavi

The Vaccine Alliance buys and stockpiles vaccines for poorer countries. It keeps an Ebola stockpile — but only for the Zaire strain, not the Bundibugyo virus spreading now, though CEPI announced a $62M fast-track of three Bundibugyo candidates on 1 June.CEPI

MSF

Doctors Without Borders — an independent medical charity that runs treatment centers in war zones and outbreaks, often arriving faster than any government.

UNICEF

The UN’s children’s agency and the world’s largest vaccine buyer; in outbreaks it moves supplies, clean-water support, and public-information campaigns.

World Bank

The money pipe. Through its Pandemic Fund it can rush financing to poor countries for treatment centers, staff, and logistics.

How it all interlocks

Put it together and a response runs roughly like this:

  1. Detect — Congo’s surveillance spots an unusual cluster of deaths and notifies the WHO (its IHR duty).
  2. Confirm & declare — labs confirm Ebola; the WHO declares an outbreak, then a PHEIC.
  3. Coordinate — the WHO and Africa CDC organize the international response and cross-border watch.
  4. Treat — MSF and national teams run treatment centers; UNICEF moves supplies.
  5. Finance — the World Bank and donors fund the surge.
  6. The gap — Gavi would ship vaccine, but none is approved for this strain.
The nugget

This machinery is real but fragile. It has no police force, runs on borrowed money, and depends on its biggest members staying engaged. That fragility is exactly why one country’s choices — the subject of Section 5 — can shake the whole system.
