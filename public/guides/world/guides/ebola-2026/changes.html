Section 5

USAID, the WHO & the Trump Administration

This is the section with a live argument inside it. In 2025–26 the United States remade its role in global health. Was it overdue reform or a dangerous retreat? Your job here isn’t to memorize an answer — it’s to weigh one.

How to read this section

The dates and dollar figures below are facts. What they mean is contested. We’ll lay out what the United States used to do, what changed, and then give you the strongest version of both sides of the debate. Notice which arguments rest on evidence and which rest on values — that distinction is the heart of thinking like an international-relations scholar.

What the United States used to do

For sixty years, two institutions anchored America’s role in global health: its aid agency and its seat at the WHO.

USAID

Created in 1961 to win Cold War goodwill, it grew into the world’s largest aid agency — about $43.8 billion in 2023, roughly three of every five U.S. foreign-aid dollars.Pew

PEPFAR

The U.S. anti-AIDS program, launched in 2003: more than $130 billion invested and an estimated 26 million lives saved — the biggest commitment any nation has made to one disease.KFF

A seat at the WHO

The U.S. was the WHO’s largest funder — about $1.28 billion in 2022–23 — and helped lead the campaigns that erased smallpox and nearly erased polio.data

The DART & GHSA

USAID’s Disaster Assistance Response Team could deploy worldwide in 24–48 hours, and in 2014 the U.S. founded the Global Health Security Agenda to help countries detect outbreaks faster.2014

What changed in 2025–26

January 2025

The new administration freezes nearly all U.S. foreign aid and signs an order to withdraw from the WHO.

July 1, 2025

USAID is shut down. More than 80% of its programs are cancelled; surviving functions move into the State Department.NPR

September 2025

The “America First Global Health Strategy” replaces multilateral aid with bilateral, time-limited agreements (MOUs) negotiated country by country.State Dept

January 22, 2026

The U.S. withdrawal from the WHO is complete. All U.S. funding ends; the WHO, which had relied on the U.S. for about a sixth of its budget, halves its management team.HHS

May 2026

As Ebola spreads, U.S. researchers are limited to a “listening only” role in WHO outbreak meetings, and several top health agencies sit without permanent leaders.CNN

The collision with this outbreak

The timing put the policy and the epidemic on a collision course — and that, too, is disputed. U.S. funding for health and aid in Congo fell from about $1.2 billion in 2024 to $67 million by late 2025.STAT Aid workers and epidemiologists told reporters the cuts helped delay detection — the outbreak already had hundreds of suspected cases when it was confirmed.NPR The State Department rejected that link as “false.” Since then, U.S. commitments have climbed: as of 5 June 2026, cumulative direct Ebola-response funding stands at more than $200 million (a $38M announcement on 5 June, $112M from ~28 May, and ~$23M earlier), alongside a broader $1.8 billion OCHA contribution (announced 14 May) that includes $350 million for DRC, South Sudan, and Uganda.State DeptKFFCNN

Handle these numbers with care

You’ll hear large death tolls attributed to the aid cuts. One widely cited estimate comes from a Boston University team that modeled roughly 332,000 deaths by mid-2025.BU A February 2026 Lancet/ISGlobal paper projects roughly 9.4 million additional deaths by 2030 in a base case — and up to 22.6 million in a deeper-cuts scenario — including about 2.5 million children under five.Lancet These are estimates from models, not counted bodies, and officials have disputed them. A careful thinker takes them seriously and labels them honestly — noting who produced each number and how.

The debate, at full strength

Below is the best case each side makes. Read both before you decide anything.

Supporters of the changes argue…

  • Aid’s record is shaky. Decades of studies find weak or no link between aid and lasting growth; real development comes from a country’s own institutions.
  • Burden-sharing is unfair. The U.S. carried a disproportionate share while other wealthy nations gave a larger fraction of their income — they should step up.
  • The WHO needs reform. Critics point to its slow, deferential handling of COVID-19 as evidence it isn’t truly independent.
  • Sovereignty first. Taxpayer money should serve Americans, and five-year deals that require partners to co-invest push them toward self-reliance rather than dependence.

Critics of the changes argue…

  • Prevention is a bargain. Stopping an outbreak at its source costs a fraction of a pandemic; smallpox eradication repays its cost every 26 days, forever.
  • The human cost is huge. Even as estimates, models pointing to hundreds of thousands of deaths demand a serious answer.
  • Rivals fill the gap. Leaving the WHO hands influence to China, which pledged $500 million and is expanding its own health diplomacy.
  • The results were real. PEPFAR’s 26 million lives are program records, not projections — and the new bilateral model is untested.
Now you decide

There’s no answer key here. To sharpen your own view, ask:

  • Which arguments rest on facts (what happened, what it cost) and which rest on values (what a country owes the world)?
  • Is global health aid best seen as charity, as national security, or as strategy? Does the answer change your conclusion?
  • Could the system be reformed without being dismantled — and who decides what “reform” means?

Check your understanding

These questions test whether you can represent both sides — not which side is right.
