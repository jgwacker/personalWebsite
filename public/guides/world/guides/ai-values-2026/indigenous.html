Section 5

Indigenous Data Sovereignty

The human-rights frame of Section 3 and the Catholic frame of Section 4 both presuppose a universal moral subject — humanity in general — on whose behalf AI norms get made. Indigenous data sovereignty is the most institutionally organized contemporary challenge to that presupposition. Its argument, advanced by Māori scholars, First Nations researchers, and Native American nations, is that community sovereignty over the data that names a people is a substantive normative claim, and that the universalist frameworks of AI governance systematically miss it.

The case material is small in dollar terms beside Stargate or the EU AI Act: Te Hiku Media's Māori-language NLP project in Northland, the Standing Rock Sioux's litigation against the Lakota Language Consortium, the Navajo Nation's experience with federal COVID data. The conceptual challenge each case poses to "global AI ethics" is much larger than its budget would suggest, because each argues that universalism, applied without attention to community-specific stakes, reproduces the extractive patterns it claims to constrain.

The CARE Principles formalized a counter-norm

The most influential indigenous data-governance framework, the CARE Principles, was published on 4 November 2020 by the Global Indigenous Data Alliance, led by researchers including Stephanie Carroll, Maui Hudson, Robyn Rowe, and Tahu Kukutai. The four principles are:

C — Collective Benefit

Data should produce benefits for the indigenous community that produced it, not just for outside researchers, governments, or firms.

A — Authority to Control

Indigenous nations have the right to govern how their data is collected, used, and shared. Sovereignty over data follows sovereignty over the people the data describes.

R — Responsibility

Researchers and data holders have responsibilities to indigenous communities — not just compliance with formal consent, but ongoing relationship.

E — Ethics

Decisions about indigenous data must center indigenous values and ethics — not impose external ones.

CARE versus FAIR

CARE was designed as a deliberate counterpoint to the older FAIR Principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable), adopted in 2016 and now treated as a near-universal norm in scientific data publishing. FAIR begins from the premise that more open data benefits everyone. CARE asks the prior question: who benefits from openness, and who bears its costs? For data about indigenous communities, "open" in the FAIR sense has historically meant exposed to extraction by external researchers, pharmaceutical firms, government agencies, and now AI training pipelines, with the community typically receiving nothing in return.

Adoption by 2026 is uneven but real. The U.S. National Science Foundation, Western Australia's mandatory health-data policy, Australia's 2024 National Indigenous Australians Agency framework, and the IEEE 2890-2025 standard adopted in November 2025 all formally reference CARE. None of the major AI laboratories — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI — has adopted CARE for training-data governance.

Te Hiku Media: the flagship case

Te Hiku Media's natural-language-processing work for te reo Māori is the most-cited example of indigenous data sovereignty in operation. Te Hiku is an iwi (tribal) radio and media organization in Northland, New Zealand. Beginning in 2018, in coordination with the surrounding community, it built one of the first serious NLP corpora for an indigenous language.

The numbers are striking. A community appeal during Māori-language week collected more than 300 hours of recorded speech in ten days. Te Hiku trained a Māori speech-recognition model on hardware it controlled, and required external researchers and firms seeking access to the data to negotiate directly with the iwi.

The most widely reported moment came when Te Hiku declined to grant Duolingo and a U.S. translation company access to the training data on commercial terms. Co-founder Peter-Lucas Mahelona's formulation, now widely quoted, framed the dispute:

"Data is the last frontier of colonization."

To formalize the terms it would and would not accept, Te Hiku developed the Kaitiakitanga License. Kaitiakitanga is a Māori concept usually translated as "guardianship" or "stewardship." The license departs from FAIR-style openness by conditioning access on relationship and reciprocity with the iwi. Variants have been adopted by other indigenous-language NLP projects in Hawaiian, Cree, and Sámi contexts.

The movement is wider than its flagship

Te Hiku is the most visible case, not the largest fragment of the movement. Four other developments matter for the international picture.

  • Canada: the First Nations Information Governance Centre. Its OCAP principles — Ownership, Control, Access, Possession — were adopted in 1998, predating CARE. The Centre has spent 2023–26 working on data repatriation from federal agencies, recovering historical health and demographic data for First Nations' own governance.
  • The United States: a turn against federal baseline standards. The Trump administration rescinded Executive Order 14112 in March 2025, which had set minimum standards for federal-agency engagement with tribal data. The Standing Rock Sioux are currently in litigation against the Lakota Language Consortium over the use of Lakota-language materials in AI training systems — the most consequential tribal data-sovereignty case now in U.S. courts.
  • The Navajo Nation's pandemic experience, characterized by the Lakota scholar Desi Small-Rodriguez as the "one-way data highway" problem.ICT News Federal agencies collected detailed health data on the Navajo Nation during COVID but did not consistently share it back, leaving the Nation's own public-health response dependent on data its government did not control.
  • The Sámi across Norway, Sweden, and Finland. The GIDA-Sápmi network and the January 2023 Tromsø conference codified Sámi-specific data governance. The Norwegian Sámi Parliament now operates a three-layer approval structure, SAMINOR, for Sámi health data that had been used in academic research without consistent Sámi consent for decades.

Recognition without administrative implementation

New Zealand's 2025 State of the Nation report on Māori data found that 14 government agencies still operate with no working definition of Māori data at all — meaning they cannot meaningfully comply with the obligations set by the Waitangi Tribunal's WAI 2522 ruling. That ruling classified data as taonga, a Māori category for treasured possessions protected under the Treaty of Waitangi. The active fight is over the gap between legal recognition of Māori data sovereignty and its administrative implementation, a gap that runs in parallel to the Māori-language-AI questions Te Hiku has raised.

The international-relations stakes

The vocabulary is unfamiliar to mainstream IR; the stakes are recognizable once translated.

  1. Sub-state sovereignty. Indigenous nations are not states in the Westphalian sense, but they are state-like: they hold land, govern citizens, and in many cases enter treaty relationships with the surrounding state. When they assert sovereignty over data, they make a claim international-relations theorists describe as sub-state sovereignty — a category that sits awkwardly inside the inherited state system and is increasingly difficult to ignore.
  2. Universalism versus particularism. The major international AI frameworks — the Council of Europe Convention, the OECD AI Principles, UNESCO's 2021 Recommendation, the papal encyclical — all speak in universal terms. Indigenous data sovereignty argues that universal frameworks systematically miss community-specific harms. The argument is constructivist in a strict sense: it contests what counts as a legitimate normative subject.
  3. Norm entrepreneurship from non-state actors. The CARE Principles emerged from indigenous-led civil-society institutions, not from states, and have diffused into national frameworks (Western Australia, the Australian NIAA, the U.S. NSF) through soft-law channels rather than treaty-making. That route is what constructivist theories of norm emergence predict and describe.
  4. The decolonial critique. Mahelona, Carroll, and other GIDA-aligned scholars argue that current AI training practices extend much older extractive patterns. Whether the international AI regime treats that critique as a legitimate normative claim or dismisses it as parochial is itself a contested question — and a measure of whose vocabulary the regime ultimately adopts.

Where this lands

Indigenous data sovereignty is unlikely to become a binding international AI treaty regime. It is highly likely to keep shaping four areas: national AI legislation, particularly in settler-colonial democracies; university and research-funding requirements; technical standards documents, of which IEEE 2890-2025 is the leading example; and the perceived legitimacy of the universalist frameworks the rest of this guide has covered. The GIDA 2026 IDSov + AI Workshop and the August 2025 "CARE Directs Us Home" communiqué point toward a more organized international indigenous AI advocacy presence in the second half of the decade.

Constructivist theory takes seriously that this kind of norm contestation can be the most consequential development of a period even when it is the least visible. The international human-rights regime began in the 1940s as a normatively articulated claim from non-state actors and took decades to become the binding architecture described in Section 3. Indigenous data sovereignty may or may not follow the same trajectory. The grounds for treating it as a serious entrant are independent of that question.

What this guide has been about

In the constructivist reading, AI's international politics is a contest over norms: which actors count as legitimate moral subjects, whose vocabulary is treated as universal, which harms are recognized, and which remedies are extended. Sections 1 through 4 traced the four main frames the international system has used to articulate that environment: disinformation as norm violation, technical provenance as norm enforcement, human rights as the established universal vocabulary, and the Catholic Church as a parallel moral entrepreneur. Section 5 closes with the frame that most seriously contests the universalist assumption shared by the other four. None of these frames will set the global AI normative order on its own. What that order eventually looks like will be cumulative — built from all of them, with the balance contested at every stage.

This guide reads the AI period through the constructivist lens. Great-Power Competition in AI reads it through a realist lens; International Cooperation on AI reads it through an institutionalist one.
