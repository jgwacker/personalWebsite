Section 4

The Pope and the Moral Frame

On 15 May 2026 — exactly 135 years to the day after Leo XIII published Rerum Novarum, the founding document of modern Catholic social teaching — Pope Leo XIV signed his first encyclical, on artificial intelligence. The choice of anniversary was an editorial argument. This section asks why a two-thousand-year-old institution has entered the AI debate, what its frame adds to the human-rights vocabulary of Section 3, and how the Vatican's intervention should be read as international politics.

Few institutions in the international system enter a contested moral question with the resources the papacy brings. The Catholic Church reaches roughly 1.4 billion adherents across every continent. It runs hospitals, universities, and NGOs in nearly every country. It has the institutional continuity that no secular non-state actor can match, and it has been articulating positions on questions of human dignity for the better part of two millennia. When the Pope teaches on AI, the relevant question for an observer of international politics is by what mechanisms the teaching travels, and against what other moral vocabularies it is heard.

The pope at the center of the encyclical

Robert Francis Prevost was born in Chicago in 1955, studied mathematics at Villanova University, and joined the Order of Saint Augustine, the Catholic religious order founded on the writings of Augustine of Hippo. He spent most of his ministry in northern Peru, first as rector of the Augustinian seminary in Trujillo for a decade, then as bishop of Chiclayo from 2015 to 2023, before returning to Rome as Prefect of the Dicastery for Bishops.Vatican News He was elected pope on 8 May 2025, the first American and the first Augustinian to hold the office. The regnal name was understood, within the Vatican and by observers, as a signal. The Holy See press director, Matteo Bruni, said within days of the conclave that the choice pointed to "the lives of men and women, to their work, even in an age marked by artificial intelligence."

Key term — Rerum Novarum (1891)

Pope Leo XIII's 1891 encyclical Rerum Novarum ("Of New Things") is the founding document of modern Catholic social teaching. It rejected both unregulated capitalism and revolutionary socialism, defended workers' right to organize, asserted a right to a living wage, and held that public authority owed a duty to protect human dignity in economic life. By taking the same regnal name in 2025, Leo XIV signaled that the encyclical to come would treat AI's effects on work and dignity as comparable in scale to what mechanized industry presented in 1891 — a parallel that is an interpretive claim, not yet a verdict.

Magnifica Humanitas, May 2026

The encyclical was signed on 15 May 2026 and released publicly ten days later.Vatican.va It opens with a proposition about technology and immediately qualifies it.

"Technology is not a force antagonistic to humanity, nor is it inherently evil. However, technology is never neutral, because it takes on the characteristics of those who devise, finance, regulate, and use it."

The text returns repeatedly to a small set of concerns. On the question of what AI is, the encyclical states that such systems "do not undergo experiences, do not possess a body, do not feel joy or pain." It treats human limitations such as illness, aging, and vulnerability not as defects to be engineered away but as conditions through which "human beings often flourish."

Work is the through-line back to Rerum Novarum. Widespread AI-driven unemployment is presented as a threat to human flourishing. "The human person is an end, not a means, and the economic order must remain subordinate to human dignity." The text names "new forms of slavery" in data-labeling work and rare-earth extraction — the invisible labor that sits beneath the visible AI economy.

The encyclical calls for the world to "disarm" AI. "To disarm does not mean rejecting technology, but preventing it from dominating humanity." The phrase asks that AI be freed from "competition-driven logic focused on geopolitical and commercial dominance" — language most commentators read as a pointed critique of the great-power AI race that the realist companion to this guide describes.

The encyclical treats robust regulation of AI affecting fundamental rights as an obligation of states, not an optional policy choice. And it turns the same demand inward, calling for an internal "examination of conscience" on how Catholic institutions — hospitals, universities, and the Roman Curia itself — use and endorse AI in their own operations.

The Vatican has been building toward this document for six years

The encyclical did not arrive without preparation. The Holy See has worked through the AI question across two pontificates.

28 Feb 2020

Pope Francis convenes the Rome Call for AI Ethics. Microsoft, IBM, the UN Food and Agriculture Organization, and the Italian government sign onto six principles. The Vatican adopts the term algor-ethics for the framework.

14 June 2024

Francis becomes the first pope to address a G7 summit, at Borgo Egnazia in Apulia. He calls AI a "cognitive-industrial revolution," demands a ban on lethal autonomous weapons ("no machine should ever choose to take the life of a human being"), and urges "urgent political action."Vatican.va

28 Jan 2025

The Vatican Note Antiqua et Nova, issued under Francis by the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith and the Dicastery for Culture and Education, distinguishes AI "intelligence," which is pattern-matching over data, from human intelligence understood as embodied, conscious, and morally responsible. The doctrinal groundwork on which Leo XIV builds.Antiqua et Nova

15 May 2026

Pope Leo XIV signs Magnifica Humanitas. The text is released publicly on 25 May 2026.

What makes the papal intervention international politics

The Catholic Church is the largest single non-state actor in the international system by membership, and that reach is what most directly distinguishes its intervention from a UN resolution or an OECD principle. Catholic hospitals, universities, schools, and NGOs operate in nearly every country. A papal normative position on AI immediately becomes guidance for hundreds of thousands of institutional actors. A circular letter from Rome lands on hospital ethics committees and university research-review boards on every continent before any treaty body has read it.

The Vatican also occupies an unusual position relative to the AI industry: it neither sells the technology nor regulates it, and its working vocabulary is human dignity rather than "responsible scaling," "national security," or the Brussels effect. That independence is rare in the present debate and contributes most of the encyclical's distinctive weight. It is reinforced by a track record on this kind of teaching. Catholic social teaching shaped twentieth-century labor politics across multiple democracies, from European Christian-democratic parties to Latin American liberation theology. The explicit Rerum Novarum parallel positions the same tradition to do similar work on AI, a claim about strategy more than an established outcome, with Leo XIV's pontificate still in its first year.

The norm-entrepreneurship reading

Constructivists trace norm emergence through a familiar sequence. A moral entrepreneur articulates a new claim; the claim diffuses through other institutions; states begin to behave as if the claim were already binding; eventually treaty law or domestic legislation codifies it. The encyclical sits at the first stage of that sequence. Whether it diffuses depends on the Church's institutional weight, on the receptiveness of the major competing moral frames (the human-rights regime, the secular existential-risk discourse, the indigenous data-sovereignty movement of Section 5), and on the willingness of governments to translate dignity claims into binding rules. None of those outcomes is settled in 2026.

Frames that compete with, and sometimes complement, the papal one

The Pope is the loudest single moral voice on AI but not the only one.

  • Secular existential-risk philosophers, including Nick Bostrom, Stuart Russell, and Eliezer Yudkowsky, frame AI's stakes through scenarios of misaligned superintelligence rather than human dignity. The frame shaped early frontier-lab safety discourse but has lost political traction since 2024.
  • Information ethicists such as Luciano Floridi and Mark Coeckelbergh argue for relational analysis of AI systems within sociotechnical contexts. The frame is useful for questions of consent, privacy, and epistemic harm.
  • The labor-rights frame, carried by the International Labour Organization and by the WGA and SAG-AFTRA strike settlements, argues for union and collective-bargaining responses to AI's effect on work. The vocabulary differs from the Pope's; the conclusions about dignity overlap.
  • Indigenous data sovereignty, the subject of Section 5. The frame most in tension with universalist AI ethics, including the Catholic version. Where the Pope speaks for humanity as such, indigenous data-sovereignty frameworks insist that communities, not "humanity in general," should govern data that names or represents them.

These frames overlap and compete; none substitutes for the others. The international AI normative environment in 2026 is the cumulative product of all of them, with the human-rights frame still carrying the most institutional weight and the rest contesting its sufficiency from different directions.

What to carry into Section 5

The Pope speaks for human dignity in general. The indigenous data-sovereignty movement argues that community sovereignty over data is also at stake, and that the universalist framing common to most AI ethics, the Catholic version included, misses the question of who owns the words and stories that name a people. Section 5 sets out that argument and its institutional carriers.
