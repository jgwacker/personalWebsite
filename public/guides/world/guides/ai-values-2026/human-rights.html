Section 3

The Human-Rights Frame

The default international vocabulary for AI's ethical limits, in the institutions that built the postwar order, is human rights. The international human-rights regime predates AI by 75 years and, in the view of the UN, the Council of Europe, and most liberal democracies, applies to it by default. This section sets out what the frame delivers as binding obligation, where it falls short, and how it is now being contested.

Among the international responses to AI, the human-rights frame is the most institutionally developed. It has bodies — the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights, the Council of Europe. It has instruments — the European Convention on Human Rights, the Framework Convention on AI. It has case law from the European Court of Human Rights. It is supported by an organized civil-society network: Article 19, Access Now, the European Digital Rights association, and others. Its limits are also institutional. It binds governments more directly than firms, is enforced unevenly across jurisdictions, and now faces a competing normative frame promoted by China.

The UN OHCHR pipeline shapes vocabulary, not law

OHCHR has been working on AI through its B-Tech project since 2019.OHCHR The project applies the 2011 UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights to digital technologies, organizing its recommendations around three pillars: the state's duty to protect rights, the corporation's responsibility to respect them, and access to remedy when they are violated. The most consequential outputs of the past two years are three.

  • November 2023: the B-Tech generative-AI foundational paper, which set out three core UNGP-based recommendations for the model developers.
  • September 2024: the UN High-Level Advisory Body on AI's final report, "Governing AI for Humanity," adopted alongside the Global Digital Compact and proposing a UN AI Office and a Scientific Panel on AI.
  • June 2025: the UN Working Group on Business and Human Rights' report to the Human Rights Council (A/HRC/59/53), the first UN expert report to address AI procurement obligations for both states and downstream business users.

None of these is legally binding. All of them shape the vocabulary that other institutions then translate into binding rules. The OHCHR's soft-law track functions, for the rights regime, as the analogue of what the NIST AI Risk Management Framework does for AI safety: vocabulary that diffuses outward and is gradually picked up by harder instruments.

The Council of Europe Framework Convention is narrower than its name suggests

The institutionalist companion to this guide treats this treaty as a cooperation success. From the rights-frame angle the picture is more complicated. The Convention has 37 signatories. The European Union ratified on 15 May 2026 as the first entity to do so. Four more ratifications, including three Council of Europe members, are required for entry into force.CoE

Civil-society critics — Access Now, Article 19, the European Digital Rights association — argue that U.S.-led negotiations softened the text in two ways that matter from the rights perspective.

  • Article 3 lets each state party choose whether to apply the Convention's principles to the private sector. Among early signatories, only Norway has declared that the principles will bind private actors operating in its jurisdiction. Most signatories have reserved the right to confine the obligations to their own governments, meaning the treaty does not, by default, regulate the firms that build the systems.
  • Article 3.2 contains a blanket national-security exemption substantially broader than the proportionality doctrine the European Court of Human Rights applies under the ECHR. A state can therefore invoke national security to exempt almost any AI use from the Convention's obligations, in ways the Court's case law under the ECHR would not allow.

The result is a binding international AI treaty that, in its default form, primarily constrains how governments use AI on their own citizens. A meaningful achievement, but narrower than the "AI under international human-rights law" framing the treaty is often given in press accounts.

The European Court of Human Rights has begun to rule on AI

The most binding human-rights case law on AI to date comes from the European Court of Human Rights, whose jurisdiction extends across all 46 Council of Europe member states. The doctrine on which subsequent AI cases now build was set in three judgments handed down over the past decade.

Bărbulescu v Romania (2017)

Grand Chamber. The Court held that workplace digital monitoring engages Article 8 of the ECHR (the right to private life) and set baseline standards of notice, necessity, and proportionality that apply directly to AI-driven workplace surveillance.

Big Brother Watch v U.K. (2021)

Grand Chamber. The Court held that the United Kingdom's bulk-interception regime exposed by the Snowden disclosures violated Articles 8 and 10 (private life and freedom of expression). The proportionality standards set here are the doctrinal basis for AI-enabled mass-surveillance cases.

Glukhin v Russia (4 July 2023)

The first ECHR judgment specifically on facial recognition. The Court found that Russia's use of facial-recognition technology to identify a protester from CCTV footage violated Articles 8 and 10. It is now the framework for the biometric-AI cases moving through the Court's docket.

These judgments bind the 46 Council of Europe member states. They are persuasive authority worldwide. Taken together they are the closest thing to AI-specific case law that has reached final judgment in any international court.

The United States is exiting the rights-frame institutions

The most consequential development for the international human-rights frame on AI during 2024–26 was U.S. disengagement from the institutions that carry it. Three moves stand out.

  • Withdrawal from the Freedom Online Coalition, formally announced 12 February 2026 following a Trump executive directive targeting 66 international organizations. The Coalition had been the principal grouping of liberal democracies — 42 members at peak — coordinating human-rights positions on digital technologies. The U.S. exit cut its membership by one and reduced its normative weight by more than that.FOC
  • Withdrawal from the UN Human Rights Council, which the Trump administration left in early 2025 as it had in 2018 before the Biden administration rejoined. The Council remains the principal UN forum where rights-frame positions on AI are developed.
  • Continued participation in the Hiroshima AI Process — the G7-led voluntary framework, with 66 country participants and 38 organizational signatories as of 2026. The administration's preference for voluntary, principle-based regimes has kept the United States inside the Hiroshima track while it exits the harder rights-frame institutions.MOFA

The institutionalist companion to this guide reads U.S. AI diplomacy under the second Trump administration in the same way: exit from multilateral and rights-frame instruments, continued presence in voluntary and principle-based ones.

China is promoting an alternative vocabulary

The Global AI Governance Initiative, launched by China in October 2023, is the most explicit normative alternative to the human-rights frame.MFA China Its three central moves are to elevate state sovereignty as the primary principle in place of universal individual rights, to make development equity the principal substantive concern, and to lodge governance authority in UN bodies under member-state control rather than independent expert mechanisms. Civil and political rights are not framed as constraints on state action.

Whether the Initiative functions as a genuine alternative framework or primarily as a blocking posture inside multilateral fora is contested among observers. Either way, its existence ends the human-rights frame's status as the default international vocabulary for AI governance. The frame is now one among at least two, and in many post-colonial and non-aligned states the Initiative's development-and-sovereignty framing has traction the rights frame's individual-protection focus does not.

Key concept — Norm contestation

Constructivists track the emergence of new norms and also the contestation of existing ones. The human-rights frame for AI was the default international vocabulary in 2022–24. By 2026, with U.S. withdrawal from key rights institutions and the consolidation of the Chinese alternative, it has become a contested frame — one among several. The Council of Europe Convention and the European Court's judgments give it real institutional weight inside Europe. The OHCHR pipeline and UNESCO Recommendation give it normative weight worldwide. The U.S. exits and the Chinese alternative mark the limits of that weight in the wider international system.
