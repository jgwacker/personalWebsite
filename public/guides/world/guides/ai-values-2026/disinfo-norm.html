Section 1

Disinformation as a Cross-Border Norm Violation

Cross-border influence operations are as old as states. What 2024 made cheap was their production at scale — and that drop in cost forced the international system to declare, often for the first time, which information practices it considers illegitimate. The substantive question of the year was not whether AI swung elections. It was which uses of AI the major democracies, platforms, and standards bodies would treat as norm violations, and how they would enforce that judgment.

Constructivist international-relations theory takes its starting point from the question of which behaviors states recognize as legitimate. International order rests on shared expectations, and a technology that lets actors violate those expectations cheaply puts the order under adjudication: tighten the expectation, enforce it harder, or accept the new behavior. AI-amplified disinformation is now inside that adjudication, and the cases below are how the international system is conducting it.

AI did not measurably swing 2024's elections

The most-cited empirical finding of the year converged across institutions normally unwilling to agree on anything. The Harvard Ash Center, MIT Technology Review, the Alan Turing Institute, and the Munich Security Conference all concluded that AI did not measurably alter the outcomes of the year's national votes.Harvard Results in the United States, the United Kingdom, India, Indonesia, France, Mexico, and roughly a dozen other elections showed none of the AI-attributable shifts that pre-election scenarios had warned about.

For constructivists the question reframes. The relevant analytical move is not whether votes moved but which behaviors the international system began to recognize — and to enforce against — as illegitimate.

The cases that mattered without moving votes

Four episodes set the agenda for what would and would not be treated as a norm violation.

Slovakia, 2023

Two days before the vote, fabricated audio of liberal candidate Michal Šimečka appearing to discuss ballot-rigging circulated inside the campaign-moratorium window. Pro-Russia Robert Fico won. HKS researchers concluded the deepfake mostly reinforced existing distrust rather than created it, but the moratorium-window release became a template subsequent operators copied.HKS

Romania, Nov 2024 (annulled)

Pro-Russia outsider Călin Georgescu rose from roughly 1 percent in October to first place, propelled by 25,000 coordinated TikTok accounts and a single operator who paid influencers around one million euros. The episode turned on algorithmic amplification rather than AI generation, but the Romanian Constitutional Court annulled the result — the first EU election overturned for digital interference.CEPA

Indonesia, 2024

The Golkar party distributed a deepfake of long-dead dictator Suharto urging votes as an official campaign asset. Winning candidate Prabowo Subianto used an AI cartoon avatar to soften his strongman reputation. Neither was foreign interference; both raised the harder question of which synthetic political content a democracy treats as legitimate art and which as deception.

Biden NH robocall, Jan 2024

An AI-cloned Biden voice told New Hampshire Democrats to "save your vote for November." A Democratic consultant commissioned the call, he said, to raise alarms. The FCC declared AI robocall voices illegal under existing telecom law; the operative was acquitted on all criminal charges in June 2025. Domestic in origin, but the episode hardened the U.S. legal posture.NPR

The frontier labs are publishing intelligence briefs

State-linked influence operations now use AI as a matter of course, and the major model providers have begun documenting them in quarterly reports that read less like corporate transparency and more like open-source intelligence.

  • OpenAI's first such report, in February 2024, named five state-affiliated actors using ChatGPT for translation, code, and post-drafting: China's Charcoal Typhoon and Salmon Typhoon, Iran's Crimson Sandstorm, North Korea's Emerald Sleet, and Russia's Forest Blizzard.OpenAI
  • By October 2025 OpenAI had disrupted more than forty distinct networks. Every report reaches the same conclusion: actors are using AI to scale existing playbooks, not to invent new ones.
  • Microsoft's pre-election threat report documented Russia's "Storm-1516" deepfakes targeting both Harris and Trump, Iranian reconnaissance against U.S. election websites, and Chinese influence narratives directed at specific Republican senators.
  • Meta's threat reports record that the majority of AI-generated content attracts almost no authentic engagement — high production volume, low reach, networks of bots responding mostly to other bots.

The norm being enforced is older than AI

The international system has long held an implicit norm — call it the non-intervention norm in democratic politics — against one state covertly trying to swing another's elections. The norm has always been violated; what made it a norm was that violations were treated as illegitimate. AI did not create those violations. It lowered their cost. The constructivist question is what the international system does with the new affordability.

Key term — Norm violation and norm enforcement

In constructivist IR theory, a behavior is a norm violation only when other actors treat it as one. The international order's responses — diplomatic protest, sanctions, formal attribution, public exposure — are what give the underlying expectation force. The frontier labs' disruption reports, the Romanian Constitutional Court's annulment, EU Digital Services Act enforcement actions, and the U.S. intelligence community's attribution statements all function as norm enforcement: each makes a particular violation visible and costly so that the underlying expectation about acceptable behavior remains in force.

What 2024 codified, and what it left open

Read together, the cases reveal an international system articulating three distinct positions — not yet treaty law, but recognizable in how states react.

  • AI-amplified election interference by a foreign state is treated as illegitimate. That virtually no state has publicly defended Russia's or Iran's 2024 operations suggests the underlying norm holds.
  • Algorithmic amplification of partisan content within a single state is legally contestable but not internationally adjudicated. The Romania annulment is the closest any institution has come to treating platform-mediated political manipulation as a constitutional matter.
  • Synthetic political content used by parties within their own elections remains normatively unsettled. Indonesia's campaign deepfakes, India's AI-translated speeches, and political cartoons generated by AI are not internationally prohibited, and whether they should be is the live debate.

None of these positions is binding international law. All three are visible in the pattern of state and institutional reaction: diplomatic protests, platform-policy announcements, regulatory enforcement, journalistic exposure. Constructivists treat exactly that pattern as evidence that the norms exist, regardless of whether a treaty has yet codified them.

What to carry into Section 2

Once the international system has identified AI-amplified information manipulation as a norm violation, the operative question becomes which technical and legal instruments it builds to enforce that norm.
