Section 4

The Soft-Law Layer

Beneath the binding instruments of Section 3 sits a far larger ecosystem of voluntary frameworks, corporate commitments, technical standards, and coordinated declarations. Nearly every state and frontier lab participates in some part of it. The analytical question, set up in Section 1, is which components function as policy and which as posture.

The post-Bletchley voluntary commitments, the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, the AI Safety Institutes Network, and the Frontier Model Forum dominate this layer. None creates binding international law. All four shape what binding international law has been written or may be written next.

Voluntary commitments are signed but no longer monitored

The most visible round began on 21 July 2023, when seven leading labs met President Biden at the White House and signed a set of voluntary commitments covering internal and external red-teaming, watermarking of AI-generated content, security investment, and public reporting of model capabilities and limitations.White House Eight more companies joined that September. Bletchley (November 2023) and the Seoul Frontier AI Safety Commitments (May 2024) extended the format internationally; Seoul added xAI and China’s Zhipu AI, raising the signatory pool to sixteen frontier labs.

What happened next

President Trump rescinded Executive Order 14110 on January 20, 2025. That order had been the U.S. government’s principal mechanism for following up on the voluntary commitments. No signatory has publicly renounced the commitments. Federal monitoring has ceased. Structurally, the regime is now signed but unmonitored. Whether the firms still consider themselves bound is a matter of corporate discretion — and a textbook cheap-talk test, since the cost of abandonment has fallen close to zero.

The NIST framework has become the regime’s shared technical language

The American soft-law contribution with the broadest international footprint is the NIST AI Risk Management Framework. The U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology published AI RMF 1.0 in January 2023, defining four core functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — and a structured vocabulary for AI risk. A Generative AI Profile followed in July 2024, adding more than 200 recommended actions specific to foundation models.NIST

The framework is voluntary inside the United States; its international influence has been disproportionate. The EU AI Act incorporated NIST RMF concepts into its risk-categorization scheme. The Council of Europe Framework Convention’s risk-assessment provisions trace to the same source. The 2024 OECD AI Principles update drew on it directly. The Singapore Model AI Governance Framework, the Japanese AI Promotion Act, and the Brazilian AI bill all reference it. The NIST RMF is the closest thing the international AI regime has to a de facto shared technical vocabulary — soft law that has acquired the practical weight of hard law.

Key concept — Soft law as norm export

The institutionalist literature has a label for this dynamic. A standard developed inside one jurisdiction becomes the conceptual foundation for binding rules in many others. Once the vocabulary is shared, jurisdictional differences narrow even where formal cooperation never occurs. The NIST RMF is the AI-policy instance of the pattern. Earlier instances include the FATF anti-money-laundering recommendations and the Basel banking standards.

The AI Safety Institutes Network is Bletchley’s most durable product

The AI Safety Institutes Network is the institutional innovation that survived the post-Bletchley fracture intact. The U.K. AISI launched in November 2023, the U.S. followed within days, and by the Seoul Summit in May 2024 eleven governments and the EU had committed to standing up institutes. The Network convened formally for the first time in San Francisco in November 2024.

Three Network functions exceed what any individual institute could deliver alone: it shares evaluation methodologies across jurisdictions, runs joint red-team exercises on frontier models, and publishes consensus technical reports that major labs find harder to dismiss than any one government’s assessment. The 2025 International AI Safety Report, co-authored by AISI personnel from 33 countries plus the EU and UN, is the most-cited example.

The political framing then fractured. In February 2025 the U.K. renamed its AISI the AI Security Institute, signaling a recalibration toward national-security risks (CBRN, cyber) and away from broader societal AI safety. In June 2025 the United States followed, renaming its institute the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI). Both rebrands retained staff and institutional home; what changed was the word “safety,” replaced in the U.S. case by “standards and innovation.” The Network continues to function technically. Whether it functions politically is the question Section 5 takes up.

The Frontier Model Forum is industry self-governance with limited evidence of effect

The Frontier Model Forum, launched in July 2023 by Anthropic, Google, Microsoft, and OpenAI, has the mandate to advance frontier-AI safety research, share best practices with policymakers, and fund external work. By 2026 it had disbursed more than $5 million in grants to independent safety researchers and coordinated intelligence-sharing on adversarial model distillation among its four members.FMF

The Forum is the regime’s clearest case of industry-led self-governance, which is to say the regime’s clearest case of regulatory positioning by the four largest U.S. labs. Members frame it as responsible cooperation; on the public evidence, the cooperation is real but mostly defensive — coordinated talking points for regulators, shared monitoring of distillation attacks, grants too small ($5M total) to shift safety research at the field level. Whether the Forum produces safety outcomes its members would not have produced individually is the empirical question, and no published evidence resolves it.

The soft-law layer carries most of the regime’s actual work

The soft-law layer is where international AI cooperation has the most volume in 2026. The binding instruments of Section 3 are real but European. The summit declarations of Section 2 set political frames but bind no one. The operational work — joint evaluations, shared technical standards, coordinated voluntary commitments, cross-border information sharing — runs through soft-law institutions invented since 2022.

This is the strongest institutionalist case for taking soft law seriously. The institutions are real even when the laws are not. Shared vocabulary makes coordination possible when treaties cannot. The voluntary commitments, individually cheap talk, have collectively built the political and technical ground on which Section 3’s binding instruments became drafting-feasible at all.
