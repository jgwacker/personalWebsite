Section 2

The Summit Process

Summit diplomacy is the most visible layer of the international AI regime. Governments have convened on AI roughly every six months since November 2023, producing four major declarations and one durable institution. This section traces that chronology and assesses, in the language of Section 1, which signatures imposed costs and which did not.

The summit layer does not produce binding obligations — that work belongs to Section 3 — but it fixes political framings, defines which governments count as inside the cooperative core, and determines which problems set the next year’s agenda. Read with the cheap-talk test from Section 1, the chronology yields a clear story about where the regime tightened and where it cracked.

Four summits, three years, one fracture

Bletchley Park — November 1–2, 2023

The U.K. convened the first global AI Safety Summit at the wartime code-breaking site, partly as national positioning and partly because no other host had stepped forward. Twenty-eight countries, including China, signed the Bletchley Declaration, a non-binding document acknowledging shared risks from “frontier AI” and committing to continued dialogue. Its one durable institutional product was the AI Safety Institute: the U.K. and U.S. each launched one within days. The substantive achievement was atmospheric — Bletchley demonstrated that the major AI powers, China included, would sit in the same room on the topic — but that fact had not previously been established.background

Seoul — May 21–22, 2024

South Korea and the U.K. co-hosted the formal follow-up, on a quieter and more technical agenda. The substantive output was an agreement among ten governments and the EU to establish a Network of AI Safety Institutes, which first convened in San Francisco the following November. Sixteen frontier labs — including xAI and China’s Zhipu AI — signed the Seoul Frontier AI Safety Commitments, extending the format established by the July 2023 White House voluntary commitments.

Paris — February 10–11, 2025

The Paris AI Action Summit was the fracture point. France hosted, fifty-plus countries — including China, India, and the EU — signed a statement on Inclusive and Sustainable Artificial Intelligence for People and the Planet, and the United States and the United Kingdom did not.Time Vice President Vance used the summit address to articulate the new American position: AI-safety regulation itself constitutes a strategic threat to U.S. technological leadership. The declaration was diplomatic cheap talk by the Section 1 test. The refusal to sign was not. It announced that the cooperative arrangement which had run from Bletchley through Seoul had ended.

New Delhi — February 16–21, 2026

The fourth summit was the first hosted by a Global South state, and it rebranded deliberately, from “AI Safety Summit” to AI Impact Summit. The shift moved the agenda away from existential risk and toward development, inclusion, and economic opportunity. Delegations came from more than 100 countries, 20 heads of state, and 60 ministers. Summits work as focal-point coordination, not as legislation

Summit diplomacy functions as a focal-point coordination mechanism: each meeting forces the relevant actors into the same room and obliges them to declare something publicly. The declarations are usually weak; the focal moments are not. Bletchley fixed “frontier AI” as the international category of analysis. Seoul placed “AI Safety Institutes” on the institutional map. Paris ended the assumption of transatlantic AI cooperation. New Delhi shifted the regime’s center of gravity away from Bletchley’s English-speaking core.

None of this is binding international law. All of it determines what binding international law (Section 3) and corporate voluntary commitments (Section 4) can plausibly demand. For institutionalist analysis, the summit layer is where political feasibility is renegotiated in public, in communiqués most observers do not read carefully.

Key term — Regime complex

International-relations scholars use the term regime complex for a set of overlapping institutions and processes covering the same issue area without a single governing authority. The summit process is one component of an AI regime complex that also includes the EU AI Act, the Council of Europe Framework Convention, the OECD AI Principles, the UN High-Level Advisory Body, the Frontier Model Forum, the AI Safety Institute Network, and bilateral arrangements between major economies. No single component governs the whole. Each governs part. The institutionalist claim is that the summit declarations supply enough shared political vocabulary for the regime complex to cohere even when no single component could.

The summit declarations were mostly forgettable. The institutions they left behind — Safety Institutes, the Network, the “frontier AI” category itself — are what the regime is now made of.

What to carry into Section 3

The summits set political frames; the binding work happens beneath them. Section 3 turns to the hard-law layer, where signatures impose real costs to break because the documents carry enforcement.
