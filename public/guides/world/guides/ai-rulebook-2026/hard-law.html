Section 3

The Hard-Law Layer

Two binding international AI instruments existed in 2026 — the EU AI Act and the Council of Europe Framework Convention — alongside one widely adopted soft-law standard, the OECD AI Principles, that shaped both. All three originate in Europe. Together they constitute the most consequential body of AI governance law produced anywhere to date.

Hard law on AI is sparse by design. Most states do not want to be bound; the European states that do must extend their reach through market access (the Brussels effect) or through human-rights instruments not specific to AI. The result is a small set of binding documents and a much larger set of voluntary ones (Section 4) that treat the binding documents as conceptual foundation.

The EU AI Act sets the global compliance floor

The European Union’s AI Act became law on 1 August 2024 and phases in over three years. It is the most comprehensive AI regulation in force and the most studied. Penalties are tiered:

  • Up to €35M or 7% of global turnover for prohibited practices (mass biometric scraping, social scoring, certain real-time biometric ID)
  • Up to €15M or 3% for most other breaches, including general-purpose-AI obligations
  • Up to €7.5M or 1% for supplying incorrect information to regulators

The phase-in:

Aug 1, 2024

Entry into force — clock starts on every deadline that follows.

Feb 2, 2025

Prohibited practices banned. AI-literacy obligations begin.

Aug 2, 2025

General-Purpose AI (GPAI) obligations apply, including Article 53’s training-data summary requirement.

Aug 2, 2026

Most high-risk-AI obligations apply. The AI Office may begin compliance verification of GPAI summaries.

Aug 2, 2027

Models already on the market before 2025 must finally comply. Stricter rules for safety-critical product AI begin.

The Act has produced substantial new institutional infrastructure: a European AI Office (February 2024) with approximately 140 staff at full strength; an EU AI Board with member-state coordination authority; an Advisory Forum with civil-society and industry seats; and a Scientific Panel of Independent Experts. None of these bodies existed in 2023. Together they form the most concentrated AI-governance bureaucracy in any single jurisdiction.

The Council of Europe Framework Convention is binding but narrow

For international-law analysis, the Council of Europe Framework Convention on AI is the central document. The Committee of Ministers adopted it on 17 May 2024, and it opened for signature on 5 September 2024 in Vilnius — the first legally binding international treaty on AI.CoE

Key facts

8 chapters, 26 articles. Covers AI’s impact on human rights, democracy, and rule of law. 37 signatories as of mid-2026 — including the U.S., U.K., EU, Canada, Japan, and Israel. The EU formally ratified on 15 May 2026, the first entity to do so. The treaty enters into force when 5 ratifications including 3 CoE members are filed — not yet reached as of May 2026.

The Convention matters because it is binding. Its substantive reach, however, is contested. Civil-society critics — Access Now, Article 19, EDRi — argue that U.S.-led negotiations weakened the text on two specific points.

  • Article 3’s private-sector carve-out. Signatories may opt out of applying the Convention’s principles to private actors. Norway alone has declared the Convention will bind private actors operating within its jurisdiction. The other signatories have effectively reserved the right to confine the obligations to their own governments.
  • Article 3.2’s national-security exemption is broader than the proportionality doctrine developed by the European Court of Human Rights. A signatory can therefore invoke national security to exempt nearly any AI use from the Convention’s obligations.

The Convention is binding in form and toothless in drafting. The private-sector carve-out and the national-security exemption together let a signatory ratify, point to the treaty in any international forum, and continue to deploy AI in ways the treaty was nominally written to constrain. Civil-society groups argue this leaves a foundation that tighter application can be built on later. Possibly. For now, the cost of signing is mostly the cost of ratification; the cost of compliance, for most AI deployed in 2026, is close to zero.

The OECD AI Principles supply the regime’s shared vocabulary

The OECD AI Principles, not a treaty but the most widely cited soft-law standard in the field, were adopted in May 2019 and updated in May 2024 to address generative AI, misinformation, environmental costs, and bias. Forty-seven adherent governments, including the EU and several non-OECD states, currently endorse them.OECD.AI

The Principles do what the harder instruments cannot: they fix the conceptual vocabulary of national AI policy worldwide. The EU AI Act cites them. The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (Section 4) was drafted in dialogue with them. The G20, G7, ASEAN, and African Union have endorsed them as baselines. In regime-complex terms, they are the connective tissue between the binding instruments above and the voluntary commitments of Section 4.

Whether the Principles produce concrete obligations depends on national translation. The OECD’s own implementation tracking shows uneven uptake: most adherents cite the Principles in strategy documents, fewer translate them into binding rules.

Europe sets the rules because no one else has tried

The binding AI law that exists in 2026 is European. The EU AI Act is European law. The Council of Europe Framework Convention is European law in international form. The OECD is headquartered in Paris and run by a secretariat steeped in the European regulatory tradition. The United States, China, and most other major economies have produced national rules but no binding international instruments. This is the Brussels effect (Section 1) operating at international scale: a continent no longer leading in compute production or model deployment has become the most consequential source of international AI law mostly because no one else has tried.

The position is fragile. The EU AI Act faces compliance challenges and political pressure from the new U.S. administration. The Convention is signed but not yet in force. OECD influence depends on continued buy-in from non-member states that may grow less willing to defer to a Paris-based body. The European pattern persists only as long as the political conditions sustaining it persist — conditions Sections 4 and 5 examine directly.

What to carry into Section 4

The binding instruments are real but limited. Most actual international AI cooperation in 2026 runs through voluntary commitments, technical standards bodies, and corporate self-governance — the soft-law layer. Section 4 asks whether that layer amounts to more than the cheap talk Section 1 warned about.
