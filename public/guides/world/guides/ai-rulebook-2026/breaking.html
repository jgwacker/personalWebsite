Section 5

Where Cooperation Breaks

The institutionalist case for the AI regime — summit declarations, binding treaties, voluntary commitments, the AISI Network — assumes continued participation by its largest members. Beginning in January 2025 the United States began exiting that regime through a sequence of formal and informal withdrawals. This section catalogues them and assesses, in the cheap-talk framework of Section 1, what each withdrawal actually cost.

Almost every institution covered in Sections 2 through 4 has lost some component of U.S. engagement since January 2025. The pattern is not random. It traces a coherent theory of AI governance — bilateral deals, voluntary commitments, and explicit hostility to multilateral safety frameworks. This section examines whether the cooperation regime can hold when one of its founding members systematically disengages.

Five U.S. withdrawals between January 2025 and February 2026

1. The Paris Summit communiqué (February 2025)

The first visible break. Fifty-plus countries — including China, India, and the EU — signed the Paris AI Action Summit statement; the U.S. and U.K. did not. Vice President Vance argued at the summit that AI-safety regulation was itself a strategic threat to U.S. technological leadership. The refusal abandoned no binding obligation, but it was the first time since Bletchley that the United States had publicly declined membership in the cooperative core.

2. Rescission of Executive Order 14110 (January 20, 2025)

The Trump administration rescinded the Biden-era executive order on AI safety within hours of inauguration. EO 14110 had been the U.S. follow-up mechanism for the July 2023 voluntary commitments and the legal basis for U.S. participation in the international AI safety regime. Rescission did not legally void the commitments themselves — they had been voluntary from the start — but it eliminated the federal monitoring infrastructure built around them. Firms wishing to walk the commitments back now faced no government counterparty.

3. AISI → CAISI rebrand (June 2025)

In June 2025 the Trump administration renamed the U.S. AI Safety Institute the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI). The institute retained its NIST home and most of its staff. What changed was the word “safety,” replaced by “standards and innovation.” Critics read the rebrand as a mission downgrade; the administration read it as a clarification.FedScoop

The practical effect has been mixed. CAISI continued model evaluations — more than forty by May 2026 — and signed new testing agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI. The political signal to the AI Safety Institutes Network was unmistakable: the United States no longer treats safety as the organizing frame of its participation. The U.K. AISI rebrand to “AI Security Institute” in February 2025 conveyed a parallel message.

4. Withdrawal from the Freedom Online Coalition (February 2026)

The United States formally withdrew from the Freedom Online Coalition on 12 February 2026, pursuant to a Trump directive targeting 66 international organizations.FOC The FOC is a coalition of 42 (now 41) democracies that had served as the principal forum for the human-rights frame on AI (covered in the Contested Norms in AI guide, §3). U.S. withdrawal weakened the coalition’s normative authority and opened political space for China’s alternative framework — the Global AI Governance Initiative, launched October 2023 — to compete for legitimacy.

5. Political withdrawal from the AISI Network (ongoing)

The AISI Network has not formally dissolved. CAISI continues to attend technical meetings. The U.S. and U.K. rebrands, combined with the Paris non-signature, have nonetheless created a measurable gap between the technical-cooperation track, which still functions, and the political-cooperation track, which has visibly fractured. The 2025 International AI Safety Report — the Network’s flagship product — appeared on schedule. The summit declarations supporting it now lack U.S. and U.K. signatures.

The regime persists — minus its enforcement bandwidth

Institutionalist theory makes a specific prediction here, and the 2026 record so far is consistent with it. International regimes survive individual member exits when they create ongoing value for the remaining members. The EU, the Council of Europe states, Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Canada, and Australia retain continuing interest in cooperative AI governance. The 70-plus states that voted for the November 2025 UNGA resolution on autonomous weapons (covered in the Great-Power Competition in AI guide, §5) show that the political constituency for cooperation now extends well beyond the United States. China itself, pursuing its own competitive logic, has continued to attend summits, sign selected non-binding documents, and advance its alternative governance frame. The regime is load-bearing for everyone else. U.S. departure has shifted the center of gravity away from Bletchley’s Anglo-Atlantic axis toward a Paris-Brussels-Tokyo-Delhi grouping; it has not ended the regime.

What it has done is strip out enforcement bandwidth. The EU AI Act binds activity inside the EU. The Council of Europe Framework Convention binds its (not yet five) ratifiers. The AISI Network produces shared methodologies but cannot enforce them. The Frontier Model Forum’s grants total under $10 million to date — useful at the margin, not transformative. The instruments that imposed real cost on frontier labs always relied on U.S. agencies — BIS, DoJ, FTC, SEC — to make the cost stick. Without them, the regime can still set vocabulary, run evaluations, and publish reports. What it cannot do is bind a firm that decides to ignore it. The institutionalist position has to live with that constraint, not argue around it.

Where this leaves the institutionalist tradition

The institutionalist case was strongest in 2023 and 2024, when major powers were converging on shared cooperative structures. It is weaker now, and the trajectory through 2026 is downward — U.S. exit is broadening rather than reversing, the Convention is signed but not in force, and the European pattern depends on a political configuration in Brussels that is itself under strain. Whether the regime can hold under that pressure is a bet the institutionalist tradition is now making in public. The 2028 U.S. election cycle and the next two years of EU enforcement will settle most of it.

This concludes the institutionalist guide. The realist account of the same period appears in Great-Power Competition in AI; the constructivist account appears in Contested Norms in AI.
