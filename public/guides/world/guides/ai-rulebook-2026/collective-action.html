Section 1

Why AI Is a Collective-Action Problem

Institutionalist international relations begins from a recurring structural problem: every state would be better off if all restrained themselves, yet each is individually better off defecting — provided the others still cooperate. AI in 2026 reproduces that problem in three distinct forms. The remainder of this guide is built on the three concepts introduced here.

The prisoner’s dilemma describes the structural trap: two AI powers acting rationally end up at an outcome neither prefers. The distinction between cheap talk and costly signals sorts the resulting diplomatic record into commitments that bind and commitments that merely perform. The Brussels effect explains why a regulator can sometimes escape the trap unilaterally by being indispensable to the firms it regulates. The rest of this guide reads the 2026 record through those three tests.

Mutual defection is the rational trap

Key term — Prisoner’s dilemma

Two players choose simultaneously whether to cooperate or defect. Mutual cooperation produces the best collective outcome, but for each player defection dominates regardless of the other’s choice. Both therefore defect, and both end up worse than they would have under cooperation. Albert Tucker formalized the game in 1950 to model Cold War arms-race dynamics. It has since been applied to almost every cooperation problem in international relations.background

Apply the structure to two states developing frontier models:

Two countries, two choices, four worlds
Other country invests in safetyOther country rushes
You invest in safetySlower; everyone gets safer AIYou lose the race; rival fields unsafe AI first
You rushYou win the race; rival’s caution doesn’t protect anyoneBoth ship unsafe AI fast

The bottom-right cell — mutual rushing — is a Nash equilibrium: a stable outcome from which no player can deviate unilaterally and improve. A 2016 Oxford-Stanford paper, Racing to the Precipice, formalized this model for AI and reached an uncomfortable conclusion: in a multi-lab race, the equilibrium level of safety investment is below what any single team would have chosen in isolation, and adding competitors lowers it further.Armstrong et al. Escaping this equilibrium is the function the international AI regime is supposed to perform.

Cheap talk distinguishes posture from policy

A statement is cheap talk when it costs nothing to make and nothing to break. Voluntary safety pledges, unenforceable summit communiqués, and corporate ethics statements typically meet that definition: they signal intent but impose no penalty for betrayal.Lawfare

A costly signal is the opposite — a commitment expensive to abandon. Treaty obligations under external monitoring, regulators with subpoena power, statutory liability regimes, and governance structures that grant outside boards legal authority to halt deployment all qualify. Anthropic’s Responsible Scaling Policy occupies the borderline: a unilateral commitment without external enforcement, but one the company would now incur substantial reputational cost to abandon.

The cheap-talk test

For each commitment that follows in this guide, the analytically useful question is what abandoning it would cost the signer. If the answer is little, the commitment is signaling. If the answer is substantial, it is policy. Most commitments fall on a spectrum between the two.

A large market can regulate unilaterally — the Brussels effect

Not every cooperation problem requires simultaneous agreement among all players. A sufficiently large market can impose its rules globally without external assent.

Key term — The Brussels effect

Columbia law professor Anu Bradford coined the term to describe how the EU’s 450 million consumers compel multinational firms to apply European rules worldwide, because operating two product variants costs more than operating one.Bradford GDPR exported European data-privacy law by this mechanism. The EU AI Act is now doing the same for AI: roughly a dozen frontier models comply with European training-data-summary requirements, and none has withdrawn from the European market in response.

The Brussels effect is the optimistic scenario for unilateral regulation. The pessimistic scenario is the race to the bottom: firms credibly threaten relocation, regulators retreat, and standards converge on the lowest common denominator. For frontier AI in 2026 the Brussels effect is winning. The European market is large enough, and frontier models concentrated enough among a dozen firms, that no major lab has withdrawn rather than comply with EU training-data-summary rules. The race-to-the-bottom story applies further down the stack — compute siting, inference hosting, model distillation — but not to the frontier itself.

Why AI is harder than the cooperation problems IR theory was built on

The concepts above apply to most international cooperation problems. AI is unusually resistant to the standard solutions, for reasons that are structural rather than contingent.

  1. The shadow of the future is short. Repeated-game theory predicts that cooperation is easier to sustain the longer players expect to interact. In a technological race where each model generation arrives months after the last, the relevant horizon collapses. Firms and governments increasingly behave as if the next eighteen months will decide the decade. That belief is partly self-fulfilling — and itself part of the cooperation problem.
  2. The technology is dual-use. A frontier model capable of drafting a marketing campaign can, in principle, also assist pathogen design. The institutions that worked for narrower categories — nuclear non-proliferation, the Chemical Weapons Convention — relied on a tractable boundary between peaceful and weaponized applications. For general-purpose AI that boundary is far harder to draw.
  3. The actors are not only states. Nuclear-weapons regimes bind states because only states possess the relevant technical capability. AI is built by private firms financed in part by state vehicles — the U.S. CHIPS Act, China’s Big Fund III, Gulf sovereign wealth funds — but governed day-to-day inside corporate structures international institutions cannot bind directly. Every AI cooperation regime confronts this principal-agent problem; few have resolved it.
