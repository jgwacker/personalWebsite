Section 5

Pacific Geopolitics

A new sovereign Bougainville would land in a Pacific more militarized, more contested, and more openly aligned along US–Australia and China lines than at any time since the end of the Cold War.

The strategic background to Bougainville's ratification fight has shifted faster than the ratification fight itself. In the eight months between Toroama's re-election in September 2025 and the parliamentary debate of June 2026, Australia signed its first new defence alliance in over seventy years; Australia and the United States committed roughly AU$500 million to expanding a naval base in PNG's Manus Province; the Solomon Islands elected a prime minister who is actively reviewing the 2022 security pact with China; France's Bougival Accord for New Caledonia collapsed; and Bougainville turned down Chinese mining capital in favour of Indian. None of those moves was about Bougainville directly. All of them shape what kind of state Bougainville could become.

The Pukpuk Treaty

On 6 October 2025 — the same day Toroama was sworn in for his second term — Australian Prime Minister Anthony Albanese and PNG Prime Minister Marape signed the Mutual Defence Treaty known as the Pukpuk Treaty in Canberra.PM&C It is Australia's first new defence alliance since ANZUS in 1951, and PNG's first ever. The treaty has not yet entered into force — both governments still need to complete domestic ratification — but its operational language is already clear.

"An armed attack on either Party would be dangerous to the peace and security of both, and each would act together to meet the common danger." — Australia–Papua New Guinea Mutual Defence Treaty, October 2025

The Pukpuk Treaty includes a citizen-recruitment clause allowing nationals of each country to serve in the other's defence forces — a level of integration that goes beyond a standard mutual-defence agreement.Al Jazeera Australia has also funded a roughly AU$500 million modernisation of the Lombrum naval base on Manus Island, the site that PNG and Australia jointly used during the Second World War and that the United States has been re-developing since 2018.PINA Manus sits roughly equidistant from Bougainville, Indonesia's Maluku, and the southern Philippines, which is why the Australian and US navies can cover all three approaches from a single forward base.

The US-PNG Defense Cooperation Agreement

The American leg of the same architecture is the Defense Cooperation Agreement (DCA) signed in May 2023. It runs for fifteen years and grants the United States "unimpeded access" to six PNG facilities, including Lombrum Naval Base and Jacksons International Airport in Port Moresby.BenarNews The package as announced in 2024 was valued at roughly US$864 million, including a US$25 million maritime training centre and jetty at Lombrum. PNG Foreign Minister Justin Tkatchenko has subsequently cited 3.5 billion kina — approximately US$880 million — in DCA-related infrastructure investment across runways, wharves, and fuel storage.Manila Times

Marape's public position, repeated as recently as 30 May 2026, is that PNG will "not allow foreign military bases." The DCA gives the United States access rights rather than bases, and the distinction is legally real. Operationally it is much weaker: the access rights cover the same facilities a base would cover, with American troops, equipment, fuel, and rotational deployments, and Marape's rhetorical line is doing political work that the underlying treaty does not support.

Solomon Islands: from Sogavare to Wale

The country most directly affected by Pacific great-power competition since 2022 is the Solomon Islands — Bougainville's nearest neighbour and a country with which it shares a chain. In March and April 2022, then–Prime Minister Manasseh Sogavare signed the China–Solomon Islands Framework Agreement on Security Cooperation, which permitted Honiara to request police and military assistance from Beijing.CN-SI relations The pact triggered a sharp US-Australian counter-mobilisation; an upgraded Pacific Partnership; a revival of the QUAD's Pacific focus; and a series of bilateral security agreements between Australia and other Pacific Islands Forum states.

The Solomon Islands' own trajectory has now turned. Sogavare was succeeded by Jeremiah Manele in 2024, who maintained broad continuity with China while accepting Australian aid. A 2025 Chinese pilot project to embed mainland-style community policing in Solomons cities produced a backlash and was suspended. In May 2026, Matthew Wale — who had publicly criticised the 2022 pact while in opposition — was elected Prime Minister.CN-SI relations Wale is reviewing the pact and has called for its full text to be made public, which it has never been. A more cautious Solomon Islands government does not undo the 2022 pact, but it complicates the regional frame of a "Melanesian China bloc" — and gives Bougainville one more potential sympathiser in any future recognition fight.

New Caledonia: the comparator that collapsed

France's parallel decolonisation process in New Caledonia has been a cautionary tale for Bougainville since 2018. The Nouméa Accord of 1998 promised three independence referendums; the third, in 2021, was boycotted by the FLNKS independence movement during COVID restrictions, producing a 96.5% No result on dramatically depressed turnout. France has treated it as conclusive; the FLNKS has not.2021 referendum

In May 2024, French President Macron's proposal to expand the New Caledonia electoral roll triggered unrest that killed thirteen people — eleven Kanaks and two gendarmes — caused over €2 billion in damage, and required the first French state of emergency in forty years and 7,000 security personnel.unrest The eventual political response was the Bougival Accord of 12 July 2025, which proposed a sui generis "State of New Caledonia" within France with Caledonian-plus-French nationality and devolved foreign-relations powers — closer to the Cook Islands free-association model than to full sovereignty.Bougival

The FLNKS withdrew support within a month. The implementing referendum, originally scheduled for February 2026, has been delayed indefinitely. A follow-on Elysée-Oudinot Accord in January 2026 has also stalled.Diplomat For Bougainville, the New Caledonia collapse weakens the "Pacific decolonisation wave" framing that Toroama had been using, and confirms — just as the parliamentary debate opens in Port Moresby — that even procedurally formal, treaty-based independence pathways can deadlock when the parent state's domestic politics refuses the result.

The regional architecture: PIF and MSG

Two regional bodies will shape what a sovereign Bougainville could plausibly join. The Pacific Islands Forum (PIF), held its 54th annual meeting in Honiara in September 2025 and produced the Ocean of Peace Declaration — a soft-power counter to the region's growing militarisation, committing members to "peace, security and cooperation."PIF communique Bougainville is not currently a PIF member; PNG holds the seat that covers it. The 2026 PIF will be hosted by Palau and is expected to take up French Pacific issues and resource access.

The Melanesian Spearhead Group (MSG) — Fiji, PNG, Solomon Islands, Vanuatu, and the FLNKS — is the smaller and more politically congruent body. Toroama announced in 2024 that the ABG would apply for MSG observer status as a first step toward external-relations capability.BenarNews Indonesia is an MSG associate member, which has historically constrained the body's willingness to support West Papuan claims; the same dynamic could affect Bougainville's path to full membership. As of June 2026, Bougainville's MSG status remains that of an observer-applicant.

Who would recognise Bougainville first?

A Yes vote on 30 August would make the recognition question moot: Bougainville would become a state by mutual consent and join the UN through PNG sponsorship. Any other outcome would put recognition at the centre of every subsequent conversation. The candidates for first recognition divide into three groups, none of them strong enough on its own to make the question easy.

The first group is sympathetic Pacific neighbours. Vanuatu has the strongest track record of supporting Melanesian self-determination claims, including West Papua's, and is the most plausible first recogniser. It has not made formal commitments specific to Bougainville.The Flame A Wale-led Solomon Islands government is a possible second. Neither has the diplomatic weight to lead a recognition cascade.

The second group is the Quad-adjacent powers. Australia is bound by its new defence treaty with PNG to a position of strict deference; it will not recognise Bougainville without PNG's consent and is the strongest backstop for PNG's preferred outcome. The United States has access rights at Lombrum, an active hub-and-spokes Pacific strategy, and no obvious appetite for a confrontation with PNG over an island most Americans have never heard of. India, freshly named the preferred Panguna partner, is the most interesting member of the group — it has growing Indo-Pacific equities, no PNG-specific commitments to constrain it, and a domestic political tradition of supporting decolonisation. Its recognition decision is not predictable from public statements as of June 2026.

The third group is the rivals to the first two. China, having lost the Panguna competition to India, has an obvious incentive to complicate any Western-aligned recognition path; its instrument of choice at the UN would be the Security Council veto, which is sufficient to keep a UDI Bougainville outside the UN indefinitely. Indonesia's position is structurally similar: it has its own active self-determination problem in West Papua and will work to deny any Pacific precedent that strengthens secessionist claims.

The likely shape of a 2027 UDI scenario is therefore one in which Vanuatu, possibly a Wale-led Solomons, possibly India, and a handful of smaller states recognise Bougainville; Australia and the United States hold off pending a PNG accommodation; China actively blocks UN admission; and Bougainville settles into a Somaliland- or Northern Cyprus-style status: self-governing, partially recognised, outside the UN, dependent on bilateral arrangements with a small number of supporters. That is not the worst outcome for a new sovereign in 2026 — Somaliland has been functionally stable for thirty years — but it is also not the outcome the 97.7% referendum vote was understood to be choosing.
