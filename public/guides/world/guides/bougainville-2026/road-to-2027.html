Section 3

The Road to 2027

The twenty-five-year peace process between Bougainville and Port Moresby has produced five signed deals on how to convert the 2019 referendum into a parliamentary ratification, none of which has yet succeeded. The current schedule has the PNG Parliament voting on 30 August 2026 — the twenty-fifth anniversary of the Bougainville Peace Agreement — and the ABG declaring independence on 1 September 2027 whether or not the vote goes its way. The constitutional question that will settle what both dates mean has never been adjudicated by any court.

The Bougainville Peace Agreement was deliberately drafted to leave the hardest decision to the end. Autonomy and weapons disposal could be implemented in the years immediately after 2001; the referendum was scheduled for a decade later; the parliamentary decision was kicked into an unspecified future. That future is now. As of June 2026 the PNG and Bougainvillean governments have signed five successive deals on how to ratify the 2019 result, each of which has held in principle and slipped in practice. The current schedule has Prime Minister Marape putting the result to a parliamentary vote on 30 August 2026 and Toroama's cabinet declaring independence on 1 September 2027. Both governments have publicly committed to the dates without having resolved the constitutional question that will decide what those dates actually produce.RNZ

Twenty-five years on one timeline

30 Aug 2001

The Bougainville Peace Agreement is signed at Arawa. Three pillars: autonomy, weapons disposal, and a non-binding independence referendum to be held 10–15 years after the first ABG election. Section 342 of the PNG Constitution is amended to give the National Parliament "final decision-making authority" over the referendum result.UN Peacemaker

2005

The Autonomous Bougainville Government is established. Joseph Kabui is elected the first president.

23 Nov – 7 Dec 2019

Bougainville votes. Polling runs for two weeks across the island and outer atolls.

11 Dec 2019

The result is announced: 176,928 votes for independence (97.7%) against 3,043 for greater autonomy, on 87.4% turnout.2019 referendum

July 2021

Toroama and Marape meet at Wabag. They agree a window of "not before 2025, not later than 2027" for independence, and target 2023 for tabling the result in the National Parliament. This is the Wabag Roadmap.RNZ

5 April 2022

The Era Kone Covenant is signed at Era Kone in Bougainville. It commits both governments to the 2023 tabling target and the 2025–2027 independence window.RNZ

2023

The tabling does not happen. Bougainville Attorney-General Ezekiel Masatt publicly accuses PNG of breaching the Covenant.NBC PNG

26 June 2025

After ten days of talks in Burnham, New Zealand, Marape and Toroama sign the Melanesian Agreement. It "recognise[s] that the Bougainville desire for independence is legitimate," sets up a bipartisan PNG parliamentary committee (chaired by Dr Allan Marat), and commits to delivering the result to the 11th Parliament via a sessional order in the first half of 2026.RNZ

5 Sept 2025

Toroama is re-elected by approximately 90,000 votes — a landslide. Sworn in 6 October with a "two-year mandate" to deliver independence.RNZ

Dec 2025

The Joint Supervisory Body confirms the result will go to Parliament by mid-2026 via sessional order.PMNEC

Early June 2026

The Marat committee tables its report. Floor debate opens. Belden Namah, Tourism Minister and a former PNGDF soldier, leads opposition to ratification — citing arms-disposal non-compliance and Toroama's use of military attire. Bougainville Vice-President Masatt calls the debate "foolish and premature."PINA

30 Aug 2026 (scheduled)

PNG National Parliament votes on ratification — the twenty-fifth anniversary of the Bougainville Peace Agreement.PNG Facts

1 Sept 2027 (target)

Bougainville cabinet has recommended this as independence day — "with or without" PNG ratification.Diplomat The date echoes the 1975 declaration of the short-lived Republic of the North Solomons.

The constitutional fight under the timeline

The schedule is the easy part. The harder part is a question that Section 342 of the PNG Constitution does not answer. The Constitution gives Parliament "final decision-making authority" over the referendum result, but it does not say what voting threshold applies. PNG argues that any decision alienating territory is a constitutional amendment in substance, which requires a two-thirds absolute majority — roughly 79 votes in the 118-seat Parliament. The ABG argues that the BPA structure contemplates a simple majority because the referendum itself has already been held. As of June 2026 the question has not been adjudicated by the PNG Supreme Court. It is a political dispute that the Melanesian Agreement attempted to bypass procedurally rather than resolve.Diplomat

Two-thirds is the number that decides everything. PNG governing coalitions are historically fluid; many MPs come from provinces with their own latent secession dynamics (parts of the Highlands, West New Britain) and are nervous about setting a low bar for separation. The Diplomat's July 2025 analysis concluded that PNG is "unlikely to ratify the referendum and allow Bougainville to secede" under a two-thirds rule. A simple-majority procedural workaround is the most plausible path to a Yes vote; it is also exactly the kind of procedural manoeuvre that opposition figures like Namah will frame as constitutionally improper.

Marape's three off-ramps

The most consequential shift in 2025–26 has been in what Marape says the parliamentary vote is actually about. Through 2022 and 2023 his position was that the referendum result would go to Parliament and Parliament would decide yes or no. Since the Melanesian Agreement, and especially since Toroama's re-election, his rhetoric has expanded the menu. The parliamentary vote, he now suggests, could approve a status that is short of full sovereignty — and three models are openly on the table.The National

Marape's case for an off-ramp

  • Roughly 95% of Bougainville's budget depends on PNG transfers and external donor support. Marape: "Economic independence must precede political independence."
  • Catalonia-within-Spain, Cook Islands free-association with New Zealand, and the four UK home nations all show that high autonomy short of sovereignty can be stable and dignified.
  • A two-thirds vote for full secession is not realistically available in PNG's parliament. An off-ramp is the only structure that wins both a parliamentary majority and ABG acceptance.
  • The Melanesian Agreement itself "recognise[s] that the Bougainville desire for independence is legitimate" — but legitimacy of a desire is not the same as ratification of a state.

Toroama's case for full sovereignty

  • 97.7% on 87.4% turnout is not a margin that admits interpretation. The 1.7% who voted for greater autonomy were a rounding error.
  • Twenty thousand dead in the 1988–98 war bought a referendum that meant something. Converting it into a high-autonomy package now would retroactively redefine what the war was for.
  • "Cook Islands free-association" sounds soft; in practice the Cook Islands cannot conduct independent foreign policy, cannot join the UN, and remain New Zealand citizens. That is autonomy renamed.
  • Bougainville has already lived under PNG autonomy for twenty years and was promised this vote at the end of it. The off-ramp framing breaks the bargain of the BPA.

Of the three models, the Cook Islands arrangement is the most consequential. The Cook Islands is in formal free association with New Zealand: it has its own parliament, runs its own domestic affairs, and is treated as a state by some international organisations and not others. Cook Islanders hold New Zealand citizenship; New Zealand handles defence; the islands have no UN seat. Free association sits in an awkward middle space between sovereignty and autonomy — a category international law recognises but does not standardise. For Marape, it is the off-ramp that most plausibly satisfies a domestic two-thirds vote. For Toroama, accepting it would mean the 2019 referendum produced something other than what 97.7% of voters chose.

The Catalonia and UK models are weaker analogies, and Marape's invocation of them is more about messaging than about substantive design. Catalonia is constitutionally subordinate to a unitary Spanish state that took criminal action against the 2017 UDI; Scotland and Wales have devolved parliaments but no international personality. Both signal "autonomy with status" rather than independence. Neither is a serious proposal for a relationship between two governments that have negotiated as equals for twenty-five years.

What the off-ramp framing leaves on the ground is two governments preparing different votes. Marape is positioning for a parliamentary motion that approves a relationship short of sovereignty, with "independence" used loosely enough to win both his backbench and ABG acceptance. Toroama is positioning to declare independence on 1 September 2027 whatever the parliamentary vote produces. The two trajectories cannot both be implemented, and the constitutional ambiguity Marape is exploiting is the same ambiguity Toroama is preparing to walk through.

What happens if 30 August does not produce a Yes

Three scenarios are live in the analytical literature as of mid-2026.Diplomat The first is a procedural re-vote: PNG re-runs the parliamentary motion under different procedural rules, possibly after a Supreme Court reference, possibly under a fresh JSB-negotiated formula. The second is an alternative-status compromise: some version of free association is voted through and the ABG accepts it as a transitional or face-saving arrangement. The third is a Bougainvillean UDI: Toroama declares independence on 1 September 2027 regardless, and tries to win recognition state by state.

The UDI route is the one the international-law section already mapped: lawful under the Kosovo standard, but not a state without recognition. Bougainville's most plausible early recognisers — Vanuatu, possibly the Solomon Islands under Matthew Wale, possibly a sympathetic Indo-Pacific bloc that includes India after the Panguna-Lloyds deal — are not, individually or collectively, the kind of sponsor that turns a UDI into a UN member. Without PNG consent, UN admission is procedurally blocked: a Security Council nomination, then a two-thirds General Assembly vote. China, with Pacific equities of its own (Section 5), would be in a position to complicate either step.

What to carry into the next section

The ratification fight is, on the surface, about a constitutional threshold and a date in parliament. Underneath it is a fight about what an independent Bougainville would do for a living. Panguna — the mine that caused the war — is also the mine that any independent Bougainville will need either to restart or to permanently retire. Toroama made his clearest signal yet on that question in January 2026, when he turned down Chinese capital in favour of Indian.
