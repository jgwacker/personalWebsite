Section 1

What Bougainville Is

An island geographically part of the Solomon archipelago, bolted to Papua New Guinea by a 19th-century colonial trade, and now negotiating its way out again.

Bougainville is the largest island in the Solomons chain — roughly 120 kilometres long, with an active volcano at each end and around 300,000 people speaking nineteen distinct indigenous languages.ABG It sits about 700 kilometres east of the New Guinea mainland and only a short boat ride from the rest of the Solomon Islands. The geography by itself makes the politics inexplicable; only the colonial history accounts for them, and only as a series of accidents that nobody at the time was trying to engineer.

How an island ended up in the wrong country

Under the Tripartite Convention of 1899, Germany ceded most of the Solomons — Choiseul, Santa Isabel, the Shortlands, Ontong Java — to British administration in exchange for British recognition of German Samoa. Bougainville and Buka stayed in German New Guinea.history After 1914 the islands passed to Australian administration; in 1975 they were folded into the new independent state of Papua New Guinea. The colonial line had survived two world wars and two changes of administering power. It had never been drawn with Bougainvilleans in mind.

That is the structural grievance behind every other grievance. Bougainvilleans share ethno-linguistic ties with the Solomon Islanders to their south, not with the Highlanders of the PNG mainland. Skin colour — Bougainvilleans are notably darker than most other PNG citizens — became a tangible marker of difference, and the slur "redskin" for mainland migrants would later be a fixture of war-era propaganda. The Tripartite Convention did not create those differences, but it bound the people on the wrong side of them to a state with which they had little in common.

A short-lived first republic

On 1 September 1975, fifteen days before PNG's own independence from Australia, Bougainvillean leaders led by Leo Hannett declared the Republic of the North Solomons.history No state recognised it. It lasted around six months and was wound up by a 1976 settlement granting provincial autonomy within PNG. The date now matters again: Toroama's cabinet has proposed 1 September 2027 as the day Bougainville next becomes independent — a deliberate echo.

The Panguna war

The compromise of 1976 held for just over a decade. The Panguna copper-gold mine, opened in 1972 by a Rio Tinto subsidiary, was generating roughly forty-five per cent of PNG's export earnings by the late 1980s while paying landowners a fraction of one per cent of revenue and degrading the surrounding rivers.Bougainville conflict In late 1988 a landowner named Francis Ona led sabotage operations against the mine; PNG responded with police, then soldiers, then a blockade of the entire island.

The war ran from 1 December 1988 to 20 April 1998 — almost a decade. PNG used Australian-supplied helicopters fitted, controversially, with weapons. Bougainville Revolutionary Army (BRA) fighters operated without resupply behind the blockade. After a 1996 stalemate, Prime Minister Julius Chan secretly contracted the British mercenary firm Sandline International for about US$36 million; when the contract leaked, PNG's own defence chief mutinied, Chan was forced from office, and Australia intercepted the mercenaries' equipment shipment.Sandline affair

Casualty estimates vary by an order of magnitude depending on whose count you use. Bougainvillean and PNG sources cite 15,000–20,000 dead, mostly from the blockade's collapse of health services; conservative academic estimates put direct combat deaths at one to two thousand plus three hundred PNGDF.conflict Both figures describe the largest conflict in Oceania since 1945. Over sixty thousand people were internally displaced. The war ended at Arawa with the Bougainville Peace Agreement, signed on 30 August 2001 — exactly twenty-five years before the date Marape has now picked for the ratification vote.

The referendum that all of this was building toward

The Peace Agreement promised three things: provincial autonomy, weapons disposal, and a non-binding independence referendum to be held between ten and fifteen years after the first election of an Autonomous Bougainville Government (ABG). The vote came in 2019, slightly outside even that generous window. Polling ran from 23 November to 7 December; the result was announced on 11 December 2019.2019 referendum

Of 181,067 valid ballots, 176,928 chose independence, 3,043 chose greater autonomy within PNG, and a thousand were spoiled. Turnout was 87.4%.

That margin — 97.7% Yes — is one of the largest in the history of self-determination referendums. East Timor in 1999 was 78.5%; South Sudan in 2011 was 98.8%; the three New Caledonia referendums of 2018–2021 never crossed 47% Yes. The closest analogue is South Sudan, and South Sudan is exactly the comparator the Bougainville case wants to avoid (Section 6).

Toroama: a guerrilla commander turned president

The current Bougainville president, Ishmael Toroama, was a BRA field commander during the war. He won the ABG presidency in 2020 promising to deliver on the referendum, and on 5 September 2025 was re-elected by roughly 90,000 votes in a landslide that the regional press described as a referendum on the referendum.RNZ He was sworn in on 6 October 2025 and announced a "two-year mandate to prepare for independence."RNZ His public position on independence — repeated in every speech since the re-election — is "bold and non-negotiable."RNZ

What to carry into the next section

The 2019 referendum settled whether Bougainvilleans want to leave. The harder question, the one international law has to answer, is whether wanting to leave entitles them to a state that other governments are obliged to recognise. The next section works through the UN Charter, the 1960 decolonisation resolution, and the 2010 ICJ Kosovo opinion. None of the three gives Bougainville a clean right to statehood on the strength of the 2019 vote alone, and the recognition fight that follows from that fact is the rest of this guide.
