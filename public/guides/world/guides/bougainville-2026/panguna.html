Section 4

Panguna and Resource Sovereignty

A copper mine that paid for Papua New Guinea's first decade of independence, started the war that killed twenty thousand people, and now holds the cleanup bill — and the financing — for any sovereign Bougainville.

Any plausible economic case for an independent Bougainville runs through Panguna. The mine in the central mountains is dormant — closed since 1989, the year after Francis Ona's sabotage campaign began — but it still contains an estimated 5.3 million tonnes of copper and 19.3 million ounces of gold, with a notional value at current commodity prices of approximately US$160 billion.NIT It is also the source of the largest environmental disaster in Oceania since 1945 — collapsing pit walls, contaminated rivers, toxic chemicals in storage — and the unresolved grievance that began the war. Resource sovereignty in Bougainville is not an abstraction. It is the same physical hole in the ground that started the conflict, that has not been cleaned up, and that an independent government will need to either reopen or definitively close.

The mine the war was about

Panguna was developed in the early 1970s by Bougainville Copper Limited, a Rio Tinto subsidiary, and operated from 1972 to 1989. At its height it was generating around forty-five per cent of PNG's export earnings — the country had been independent for less than a decade and Panguna was, in a real sense, the fiscal foundation of the new state.conflict Landowner royalties were a fraction of one per cent of revenue. Tailings flowed into the Jaba and Kawerong rivers. Mainland workers — "redskins" in war-era propaganda — outnumbered locals at the mine, and the social tension that produced was deliberately stoked by both sides as the conflict escalated. The war was triggered by Panguna; the Peace Agreement could not have been signed without addressing Panguna; and the question of what happens to Panguna next is a referendum on whether Bougainville has learned anything from twenty-five years of negotiating with its own history.

The 2024 Legacy Impact Assessment

For the first three decades after the mine closed, no one had a comprehensive picture of what it had left behind. That changed on 6 December 2024, when the independent Panguna Mine Legacy Impact Assessment (PMLIA), conducted over two years by the engineering firm Tetra Tech Coffey, was released to the public.Rio Tinto Its findings were not subtle.

PMLIA findings — December 2024
  • "Imminent, life-threatening risks" from collapsing pit walls, levees, and abandoned infrastructure.
  • Ongoing contamination of the Jaba and Kawerong river systems, with waste continuing to migrate into new areas thirty-five years after closure.
  • Mine-related flooding affecting downstream access to drinking water, food gardens, and sacred sites.
  • Toxic chemicals still held in storage and contaminating soil across the broader mine area.

Rio Tinto formally accepted the findings. Chief Executive Kellie Parker said the company "recognise[s] the gravity of the impacts identified." In November 2024 Rio Tinto, BCL, and the ABG signed a Memorandum of Understanding establishing a Roundtable on remediation; the first meeting was held in Port Moresby on 6 March 2025.Rio Tinto What Rio Tinto has not done — and what indigenous-rights and human-rights groups continue to press for — is commit to a specific dollar figure for cleanup. The Roundtable is a process without a funded settlement, and eighteen months in no dollar figure for cleanup has been agreed.NIT

The Bougainville Mining Act of 2015

The legal architecture for any future mining was rewritten in 2015 and is unusual by global standards. The Bougainville Mining Act 2015 vests ownership of minerals on customary land with the customary landowners themselves — not the territorial government, and not the state.Trajectory Landowners hold a veto over both exploration and mining licences. The arrangement is a direct response to the Panguna grievance, which was a grievance about a state and a foreign company extracting value from land that belonged, in customary terms, to people who were paid almost nothing for it. The 2015 Act tries to make that pattern legally impossible to repeat.

In practice the Act means that no restart of Panguna can happen without explicit consent from the Panguna landowners — currently led by Philip Miriori as chairman of the landowner umbrella body. That consent has not been given and has not been definitively withheld either. The landowners' public position is that they will not accept a partner with a poor environmental record, and they have specifically named Chinese state-owned mining capital as falling within that category.

January 2026: choosing India over China

The decision that made global mining headlines came in January 2026. Toroama publicly directed Bougainville Copper Limited to cease discussions with China Molybdenum Company (CMOC) — one of the world's largest copper producers, with operations across Africa and Latin America — and instead pursue Lloyds Metals and Energy Limited, a mid-cap iron-ore and steel firm based in Maharashtra, as the preferred partner for a "contract mining or services partnership model."Mining.com BCL's exploration licence, granted in 2024, was left undisturbed.

The Lloyds choice is striking on three levels. The first is strategic. Choosing India over China at this moment in Pacific geopolitics is a public alignment with the Quad-adjacent bloc that includes Australia, Japan, and the United States. It does not reverse Bougainville's neutrality, but it forecloses the most obvious financing path for a multibillion-dollar copper restart at exactly the moment the ABG most needs the revenue. The second level is reputational. CMOC has been credibly accused of environmental damage at its Tenke Fungurume operation in the Democratic Republic of Congo, and the Panguna landowners' chairman publicly cited environmental performance as the basis for the rejection.NIT Section 5 of the Mining Act 2015 made that judgement available to the landowners; they used it.

The third level is the open question: whether Lloyds can actually finance and execute the restart. Panguna is a complex, large-scale copper project that has been dormant for thirty-five years; restarting it will likely require billions of dollars of capital and decades of remediation alongside production. Lloyds is a mid-cap firm whose track record is in Indian iron ore and steel, not in copper outside South Asia. The decision signals what kind of partner Bougainville will accept without demonstrating that such a partner exists at the scale Panguna requires. The financing gap that the CMOC rejection has opened is real, and it is the strongest argument Marape will use to support the off-ramp framing of "economic independence first."

Three things the ABG wants, in tension

The Bougainville government's public position is consistent but internally contested. It wants, in order of priority:

  1. Full remediation of the Panguna site, funded by Rio Tinto and PNG. The Roundtable is a step; a dollar figure is not.
  2. Restart of the mine on landowner-aligned terms, to generate the budget revenue that any sovereign Bougainville will need. The Lloyds decision is the chosen path; whether it is a viable one is open.
  3. Landowner consent driving the timeline. The 2015 Act makes that consent legally decisive; it also gives landowners a veto that may slow any restart well past the 2027 independence target.

The three goals point in different directions. Full remediation slows restart. Restart requires capital that landowner consent restricts. Landowner consent will not be given quickly enough to fund the early years of a 2027 sovereign state. The compromise the ABG has chosen — Lloyds rather than CMOC, contract-mining model rather than equity sale, remediation in parallel rather than as a precondition — is reasonable on each axis taken individually. Whether it survives contact with the actual financial mathematics of a copper restart is the question that the next eighteen months will answer.

What to carry into the next section

The Panguna choice was not made in isolation. It was made at a moment when Australia and Papua New Guinea had just signed Australia's first new defence treaty in seven decades, when the United States was actively expanding its access to PNG bases, when the Solomon Islands had just elected a prime minister sceptical of the 2022 China pact, and when France's New Caledonia accord had collapsed. Section 5 sets out the Pacific geopolitics around a possible new sovereign Bougainville — which is to say, the contest the new state would land in.
