Section 6

Four Comparators

Bougainville's process is unusual in its margins and its mandate, but it is not unprecedented. Four other cases — East Timor, South Sudan, New Caledonia, and Catalonia — set out the range of post-referendum trajectories the island may follow, and the constraints under which each ended up where it did.

Every modern self-determination case is partly defined against the others. Quebec referendums were shaped by the lawyers who had read about Scotland; Catalonia's UDI was shaped by what its leaders had absorbed from Kosovo; New Caledonia's three-referendum sequence was deliberately modelled on the Northern Ireland approach. The cases below are the four that Bougainvillean and PNG officials reach for most often in interviews — and the four whose lessons map most directly onto the choices on the table.

East Timor — the success

Portuguese Timor was invaded by Indonesia in December 1975 and annexed in 1976. The United Nations never recognised the annexation. After President Suharto's fall in 1998 and President B.J. Habibie's offer of an autonomy referendum, a UN-supervised vote was held on 30 August 1999: 78.5% chose independence on roughly 98% turnout.1999 referendum Pro-Indonesian militia violence followed; the Australian-led INTERFET mission deployed in September, and UNTAET assumed direct administration in October. Independence followed on 20 May 2002. José Ramos-Horta, Nobel laureate and current president, was re-elected in 2022. On 26 October 2025, Timor-Leste was admitted as ASEAN's eleventh member.Al Jazeera

For Bougainville, East Timor is the closest parallel — a contested overseas referendum followed by negotiated transition under international supervision. But the East Timor settlement depended on three conditions that are not present today. The first was Indonesia's domestic political crisis after Suharto; PNG is not in comparable crisis. The second was Australian military deployment under a UN Chapter VII mandate; Australia is now constrained by the Pukpuk Treaty. The third was explicit UN administration during the transition; no UNTAET-equivalent is contemplated for Bougainville. The East Timor lesson for Bougainville is that the parallel case can work — and that it required a particular combination of circumstances to do so.

South Sudan — the warning

The Comprehensive Peace Agreement of January 2005 ended the Second Sudanese Civil War. The independence referendum of 9–15 January 2011 produced 98.83% for independence — an even higher margin than Bougainville's. South Sudan became the world's newest state on 9 July 2011.2011 referendum Civil war broke out within thirty months between forces loyal to President Salva Kiir and former Vice-President Riek Machar. The 2018 Revitalized Agreement on the Resolution of the Conflict in South Sudan (R-ARCSS) created a power-sharing transitional government.

That government is now collapsing. In March 2026, the UN Commission on Human Rights in South Sudan warned that the country is "moving to total war." Machar is under house arrest; Kiir has dismissed 47 Machar-aligned MPs; the December 2026 elections have been postponed multiple times.JURIST South Sudan is the warning that an overwhelming independence vote does not solve internal political contradictions. For Bougainville, the implication is that 97.7% does not guarantee internal cohesion after independence. Bougainville's own pre-independence settlement — between former BRA factions, between landowner blocs at Panguna, between mainland-island elites and outer-atoll communities — matters at least as much for the post-2027 state as the parliamentary vote in Port Moresby does for whether 2027 happens at all.

New Caledonia — the procedural variant

The Nouméa Accord of 1998 provided for three independence referendums spaced across two decades. The first, in 2018, was 43.3% Yes; the second, in 2020, was 46.7% Yes; the third, in 2021, was 96.5% No on dramatically depressed turnout after the FLNKS independence movement boycotted it during COVID restrictions.2021 referendum France's position is that the 2021 result settled the question; the FLNKS rejects the legitimacy of a referendum it boycotted, and continues to press for a fresh vote under different rules. The 2024 unrest, the Bougival Accord of July 2025, and the failed Elysée-Oudinot follow-on of January 2026 all flow from that disagreement.

New Caledonia is Bougainville's procedural shadow. Both cases have followed a treaty-based pathway. Both rely on the parent state's parliament for final ratification. Both contain unresolved questions about who is entitled to vote and what threshold counts as decisive. Bougainville's process is in some ways more procedurally formal than New Caledonia's — a single referendum with an overwhelming result rather than three referendums with mixed results — but it is similarly vulnerable to the parent-state's domestic politics. The lesson from New Caledonia is not that the procedural path fails; it is that following every legal step does not, on its own, deliver a state.

Catalonia — the blocked case

The Catalan independence movement held a unilateral referendum on 1 October 2017 after the Spanish Constitutional Court declared the vote unconstitutional. Turnout was about 43% in a poll disrupted by police action and a unionist boycott; roughly 90% of those who voted chose independence. The Catalan Parliament declared independence on 27 October 2017 by 70 votes to 10. Prime Minister Mariano Rajoy invoked Article 155 of the Spanish Constitution the same day, dismissing the Catalan executive and dissolving the parliament; the Spanish Senate authorised the move 214 to 47.crisis Independence leaders were prosecuted; Carles Puigdemont fled to Belgium. No state recognised the UDI.

The 2024 Spanish Amnesty Law covered most participants and was upheld by the Constitutional Court 6 to 4 on 26 June 2025; the Supreme Court has held that the amnesty does not apply to Puigdemont and others on misuse-of-funds charges.amnesty law Catalonia is the case in which a UDI without parent-state consent and without a major-power sponsor produced legal cooptation rather than sovereignty. For Bougainville the implication is structural rather than rhetorical: a UDI on 1 September 2027 without PNG consent, and without a sponsor on the order of Australia or the United States, lands much closer to Catalonia than to East Timor — partial recognition, no UN seat, and a recurring legal vulnerability through whatever residual PNG instruments remain in force.

What the four cases jointly suggest

Taken together the four cases trace the range of outcomes the procedural path can produce. East Timor delivered full sovereignty, on a combination of conditions — Indonesian domestic crisis, Chapter VII Australian deployment, direct UN administration — that will not be present for Bougainville in 2027. South Sudan delivered independence and then failed to hold the new state together; that is a possibility no Bougainvillean settlement has yet had to take seriously. New Caledonia exhausted the entire procedural sequence and is still inside France. And Catalonia, having abandoned the procedural sequence in favour of a unilateral declaration, was captured legally rather than recognised internationally.

Read together, the four cases suggest a narrow corridor for Bougainville. The East Timor outcome — full sovereignty by 1 September 2027 with PNG consent and UN admission — requires PNG's parliament to vote yes by an unusually large margin and is therefore dependent on Marape's procedural strategy succeeding. The Catalonia outcome — UDI without major-power recognition — is the path of least diplomatic effort and the path of least durable result. The most realistic landing zone sits between New Caledonia's deadlock and Cook Islands-style free association: a delayed or staged independence under a free-association framework, with full sovereignty deferred to a later vote that may or may not come. That is the modal scenario on the evidence available in June 2026 — and a long way from what 97.7% on 87.4% turnout was understood to be choosing.
