<p>
I grew up in a household that revered the Constitution. The document was treated as a work of near-perfect design: the separation of powers, the Bill of Rights, the amendment process. The assumption was that if everyone followed the rules, the system would work. What I've come to believe, after years of watching the system strain, is that the rules are thinner than we were taught. The Constitution establishes the skeleton of the American regime. But much of the musculature and nervous system, the parts that make the skeleton move, was never constitutionalized at all. It lives in ordinary statutes, chamber rules, executive orders, judicial doctrines, and, most precariously, in norms of self-restraint that no law enforces.
</p>

<p>
That was manageable while norms were strong and political actors exercised forbearance. It becomes dangerous when polarization rises, judicial doctrine shifts, and actors exploit every gap left open by the sparse constitutional text. The United States has not amended its Constitution with a genuinely new structural idea since the 26th Amendment lowered the voting age in 1971. The 27th Amendment, ratified in 1992, was originally proposed in 1789. For over half a century, the world's oldest continuous constitutional democracy has done essentially no constitutional maintenance.
</p>

<p>
What follows is a catalog of 50 aspects of federal governance that are established at too weak a level relative to their importance. Some are under-entrenched: too important to rest on ordinary statute or norm. Some are overbroad: constitutional powers with too few constraints. Some are under-specified: operative meanings supplied by practice rather than text. Some are norm-dependent: arrangements that work only when actors exercise restraint. Some are patchwork: cobbled together from incompatible layers of law across jurisdictions. These are different pathologies, and they require different remedies. But they share a common diagnosis: the American regime has been coasting on institutional habits while the infrastructure beneath those habits erodes.
</p>

<h2 id="skeleton">The Skeleton and the Nervous System</h2>

<p>
Consider the hierarchy of legal durability in the American system, from weakest to strongest: informal norm → party practice → agency practice → chamber rule → ordinary statute → framework statute → judicial doctrine → constitutional text → constitutional text plus entrenched practice. The most durable settlements are constitutional amendments. The least durable are the unwritten expectations that political actors will behave responsibly. Between these extremes lies most of what actually makes the government function.
</p>

<p>
The Electoral College is the clearest example. Article II and the Twelfth Amendment describe a system of state-appointed electors, not a national popular vote for president. The modern expectation that voters choose the president directly is carried almost entirely by state law and party practice. The Constitution itself does not require it. The National Archives notes that when a person votes in the general election, that person is "not actually voting for President" but helping determine the state's electors. For something this central to democratic legitimacy, the legal foundation is remarkably thin.
</p>

<p>
Political parties are another. They are indispensable to every aspect of American self-government: nomination, coalition-building, legislative organization, presidential selection. Yet parties are absent from the constitutional text. Their role is mediated through election statutes, FEC rules, and informal practice. A system so dependent on parties but so weakly constitutionalized is structurally unstable.
</p>

<p>
Roe v. Wade illustrated a different failure mode. For nearly fifty years, the United States treated a matter of enormous social consequence as secured chiefly by a court-created constitutional doctrine rather than by a durable national statute or an amendment. When the Court's composition changed, the protection disappeared without any amendment process. The vulnerability was structural, not ideological: rights that depend primarily on judicial inference are less durable than rights encoded in statute or constitutional text.
</p>

<p>
Federal budgeting shows yet another pattern. The government's most basic function, funding its own operations, routinely fails. In all but 3 of the last 30 years examined by GAO, Congress used continuing resolutions rather than completing appropriations on time. Brookings has characterized the appropriations process as being in a "state of collapse." A government that cannot reliably fund itself is under-institutionalized in its most basic task.
</p>

<details class="mb-6 border border-base-border rounded-xl" open>
<summary class="cursor-pointer px-5 py-4 select-none">
<span class="text-xl font-semibold tracking-tight">I. Democratic Legitimacy and Representation</span>
</summary>
<div class="px-5 pb-4">
<p>
These are the rules that convert popular will into federal authority. They are the regime's input layer, and many of them are weaker than any voter assumes.
</p>

<p>
<strong>1. The effective method of presidential election.</strong> The Constitution describes electors chosen by state legislatures, not a national popular vote. The modern democratic meaning is supplied by party practice and state election law. For the most important single democratic act in the system, the legal foundation is a patchwork of state statutes layered over an 18th-century constitutional form.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a constitutional amendment establishing direct popular election of the president, or at minimum a national statutory framework that makes the democratic expectation legally binding.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>2. The norm that electors merely transmit the popular result.</strong> Even today, elector behavior is constrained mostly by state law and modern expectation rather than by the constitutional design. Some states have passed faithless-elector laws, upheld by the Supreme Court in Chiafalo v. Washington (2020), but a core stage of presidential election still depends heavily on convention.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>constitutionalize the expectation that electors are bound.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>3. Electoral-vote counting and certification.</strong> The Constitution provides sparse structure for counting electoral votes. The old Electoral Count Act of 1887 was exposed as dangerously ambiguous in January 2021. The Electoral Count Reform Act of 2022 improved procedures, but the underlying constitutional text remains thin for a core transfer-of-power function.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>constitutionalize the certification process with clearer procedures and narrower grounds for objection.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>4. Presidential transition and handoff capacity.</strong> Peaceful and competent transfer of power depends on statutes, funding rules, agency cooperation, and norms. The Presidential Transition Act provides a framework, but much of the actual handoff remains operational and voluntary.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger superstatutory transition protections with enforcement mechanisms.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>5. Federal-state boundary in election administration.</strong> The national government depends on state-run machinery for federal electoral legitimacy. The architecture is a patchwork of constitutional text, federal statutes, and 50 different state regimes. For something as basic as counting and certifying federal elections, the settlement is thinner and more heterogeneous than is healthy.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>national election standards for federal races, with federal funding and oversight.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>6. The role of political parties.</strong> Parties are central to every aspect of representation but absent from the Constitution. Their legal status is mediated through election law and First Amendment doctrine.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a quasi-constitutional statutory framework acknowledging parties' role and imposing transparency and governance standards.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>7. The primary-election system.</strong> The mechanism by which the country effectively selects its presidents and much of Congress is a statutory and party-created accretion. Given its centrality, leaving the nomination gateway to such a patchwork is a major weakness.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a national statutory framework for federal primaries with consistent rules.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>8. Campaign-finance architecture.</strong> Modern elections are mediated through an immense campaign-finance regime built by statute, regulation, and shifting constitutional case law. Because elections are the principal mechanism of democratic accountability, having the funding architecture hinge on judicial doctrine is structurally precarious.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a constitutional amendment granting Congress clear authority to regulate campaign spending.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>9. Census and apportionment machinery.</strong> The Constitution requires an enumeration and apportionment, but the detailed methods are statutory and administrative. House representation and Electoral College votes depend on this machinery.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger statutory protections for census independence and methodology.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>10. Loser's consent and acceptance of electoral defeat.</strong> Constitutional democracies require that losers accept results and that actors exercise forbearance. This is the most important extra-legal element of the system, and it is a norm only. Norm-dependence score: 4/4.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>statutory and procedural reinforcements that make contesting legitimate results more costly and less ambiguous.</p></details>
</p>
</div>
</details>

<details class="mb-6 border border-base-border rounded-xl" open>
<summary class="cursor-pointer px-5 py-4 select-none">
<span class="text-xl font-semibold tracking-tight">II. Congressional Capacity and Legislative Self-Government</span>
</summary>
<div class="px-5 pb-4">
<p>
These concern whether Congress can actually govern as a legislature rather than as an episodic veto chamber.
</p>

<p>
<strong>11. Regular appropriations and budgeting.</strong> GAO has found that continuing resolutions create inefficiencies, reduced productivity, and higher costs. In the vast majority of recent years, Congress has failed to complete appropriations on time.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>automatic continuing resolution or government-funding default that prevents shutdowns when Congress misses deadlines.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>12. The debt ceiling.</strong> The government's capacity to honor already-authorized obligations depends on an awkward statutory ceiling rather than a structural fiscal rule. This creates avoidable regime-risk around ordinary creditworthiness.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>repeal the debt ceiling entirely, or link it automatically to enacted appropriations.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>13. Impoundment control.</strong> Whether the president may decline to spend appropriated funds is foundational to legislative supremacy over the purse. The Impoundment Control Act exists but has been tested repeatedly.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger statutory enforcement with automatic judicial review and expedited resolution.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>14. OMB apportionment power.</strong> Congress appropriates money, but OMB controls the timing and distribution of spending through the Antideficiency Act. This gives the executive a quiet lever to alter implementation of enacted law.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer statutory constraints on apportionment discretion with GAO oversight.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>15. The Senate filibuster.</strong> Among the most consequential features of modern lawmaking, yet it is a chamber rule, not a constitutional requirement. A device with such vast power over national governance rests on a much weaker legal basis than its practical significance would suggest.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>either constitutionalize it (with defined exceptions) or eliminate it by rule change.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>16. Pocket-veto mechanics.</strong> The veto power is constitutional, but the pocket veto turns on whether Congress's adjournment prevents return of a bill. The trigger remains dependent on procedural circumstance and historical interpretation.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer statutory or constitutional settlement of when pocket vetoes apply.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>17. Congressional Review Act.</strong> A major tool for nullifying agency rules, yet it is an ordinary statute with a short review window. A consequential part of the legislative-administrative balance rests on a weak footing.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>redesign with clearer timelines and limited retroactive reach.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>18. Congressional oversight enforcement.</strong> Congress has investigatory power, but enforcement of subpoenas is weak, slow, and often dependent on executive-branch prosecutors or prolonged litigation.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>statutory inherent-contempt authority with civil fines, or an independent enforcement mechanism.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>19. Congressional incapacity.</strong> The Constitution has no clean mechanism for removing a member who becomes functionally unable to serve, as the Feinstein episode illustrated. Expulsion requires a two-thirds vote.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a narrowly drawn incapacity process, probably by constitutional amendment, with independent medical review and supermajority confirmation.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>20. Congressional self-discipline.</strong> Much of the actual self-dealing risk for members is left to internal rules and ordinary law.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger superstatutory ethics framework with independent enforcement.</p></details>
</p>
</div>
</details>

<details class="mb-6 border border-base-border rounded-xl" open>
<summary class="cursor-pointer px-5 py-4 select-none">
<span class="text-xl font-semibold tracking-tight">III. Presidential Power: Scope, Abuse, and Constraint</span>
</summary>
<div class="px-5 pb-4">
<p>
These concern powers concentrated in the presidency and the relative weakness of limiting structures.
</p>

<p>
<strong>21. The general scope of presidential power.</strong> Article II vests "the executive Power" in the president, but much of what that means is supplied by judicial gloss, historical practice, and interbranch contest. For the central office of the system, the constitutional specification is remarkably thin.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer doctrinal settlement through framework legislation defining the boundaries of executive action in recurring contexts.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>22. The pardon power.</strong> Textually broad, procedurally unconstrained, and carrying serious anti-accountability hazards. A president can pardon allies and subordinates whose silence benefits him. The self-pardon question has never been definitively resolved. This is a monarchical carryover with too little republican redesign.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>constitutional amendment requiring disclosure of reasons, prohibiting self-pardons, and imposing a waiting period for pardons of executive-branch officials.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>23. War powers.</strong> The constitutional allocation between Congress and the president has been supplemented by ambiguous practice and contested readings. The War Powers Resolution has not produced a durable settlement.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a new war powers framework statute with automatic sunset provisions and mandatory congressional votes within defined timelines.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>24. Emergency powers.</strong> The National Emergencies Act allows the president to unlock large bodies of statutory authority through a declaration that can be renewed repeatedly. Congressional termination is politically difficult.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>automatic expiration of emergency declarations after 30 days without affirmative congressional vote; narrower statutory authorities tied to specific emergencies.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>25. Classification and declassification authority.</strong> Vast areas of national-security secrecy are structured through executive order and administrative practice. For something that governs what the public and Congress can know, the legal basis is unusually soft.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>statutory framework for classification with mandatory review timelines and congressional access guarantees.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>26. Anti-corruption rules for the presidency.</strong> Presidential ethics architecture depends on disclosure rules, conflict statutes of partial application, and political sanction rather than a coherent framework.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>comprehensive presidential ethics statute with mandatory financial disclosure, blind trusts, and independent enforcement.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>27. Emoluments safeguards.</strong> The constitutional text prohibits foreign and domestic emoluments, but key terms remain contested and enforcement architecture is thin. No definitive Supreme Court ruling on the merits exists.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>implementing statute with clear definitions, disclosure requirements, consent procedures, and a cause of action.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>28. Impeachment standard.</strong> "High Crimes and Misdemeanors" is intentionally broad but its practical operation has become overwhelmingly political. For the only constitutional mechanism short of elections for addressing grave presidential misconduct, the standard is far less determinate than the system needs.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a quasi-constitutional body of standards developed by Congress, analogous to how the Supreme Court has developed First Amendment doctrine, rather than a rigid definition.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>29. Presidential records.</strong> The preservation of executive documentary memory is governed through the Presidential Records Act, not deep constitutional entrenchment.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger statutory protections with criminal penalties for destruction and automatic transfer to NARA.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>30. Presidential incapacity.</strong> The Twenty-Fifth Amendment addresses inability, but the operative standard remains open-textured and politically fraught.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>implementing legislation creating an independent medical commission with advisory capacity, triggered by defined criteria.</p></details>
</p>
</div>
</details>

<details class="mb-6 border border-base-border rounded-xl" open>
<summary class="cursor-pointer px-5 py-4 select-none">
<span class="text-xl font-semibold tracking-tight">IV. Appointments, Succession, and Executive Personnel</span>
</summary>
<div class="px-5 pb-4">
<p>
These concern who exercises executive power and under what accountability.
</p>

<p>
<strong>31. Presidential succession beyond the Vice President.</strong> The line of succession below the VP is statutory. Given the stakes, this is less entrenched than regime continuity requires.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>constitutional amendment fixing the basic succession structure.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>32. Appointment and temporary control of executive offices.</strong> The modern executive depends heavily on acting officials, vacancies statutes, and contested officer/employee classifications.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer constitutional-doctrinal settlement of what constitutes an "officer" requiring confirmation.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>33. Acting-government through vacancies law.</strong> A large share of executive power can be exercised for substantial periods by officials never Senate-confirmed to that role.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>strict time limits on acting service (90 days), with automatic loss of authority if no nominee is submitted.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>34. Recess appointments.</strong> The scope of recess-appointment authority has depended on historical practice, OLC interpretation, and litigation.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer doctrinal limitation, codifying NLRB v. Noel Canning.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>35. Removal protections and agency independence.</strong> Whether agencies are insulated from presidential removal depends on unstable judicial doctrine.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer constitutional-statute settlement specifying which agencies warrant independence and what removal protections attach.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>36. Civil-service political neutrality.</strong> The Hatch Act and merit-system rules maintain the boundary between professional administration and partisan machinery. For a modern state, this boundary is crucial and somewhat under-entrenched.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>superstatutory merit protections with stronger enforcement and whistleblower shields.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>37. Inspector general independence.</strong> IGs are central to anti-corruption oversight but are creatures of statute, vulnerable to vacancies and acting arrangements.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger statutory independence with for-cause removal protections and mandatory vacancy-filling timelines.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>38. The National Security Council system.</strong> The NSC is one of the main coordinating centers of national-security government, yet its real power depends on presidential organization and staff practice rather than entrenched architecture.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer statutory framework defining the NSC Advisor's role and accountability to Congress.</p></details>
</p>
</div>
</details>

<details class="mb-6 border border-base-border rounded-xl" open>
<summary class="cursor-pointer px-5 py-4 select-none">
<span class="text-xl font-semibold tracking-tight">V. The Administrative State</span>
</summary>
<div class="px-5 pb-4">
<p>
These concern the gap between the sparse constitutional text and the machinery through which the government actually governs.
</p>

<p>
<strong>39. Delegation of legislative power to agencies.</strong> Congress routinely enacts broad statutes and leaves enormous policy content to administrative interpretation. The gap between formal theory and operating reality remains one of the central under-settled features of the regime.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer congressional delegation standards with mandatory sunset provisions for major rules.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>40. Administrative adjudication.</strong> A large amount of coercive federal decision-making occurs in agency proceedings rather than Article III courts. The constitutional footing remains contested.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>clearer statutory framework for when administrative adjudication is appropriate, with stronger due-process protections.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>41. Administrative-state accountability structure.</strong> The Constitution does not map the modern administrative state. Its organization depends on statutes, delegations, removal doctrine, and judicial interpretation.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a coherent framework statute defining the relationship between agencies, the president, and Congress.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>42. The Office of Legal Counsel.</strong> OLC opinions function as the executive branch's internal constitutional law, but that role is weakly formalized. Extraordinary practical weight rests on a relatively under-entrenched institution.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>statutory framework requiring publication of significant OLC opinions and congressional notification.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>43. Federal advisory committees.</strong> Much policymaking depends on advisory bodies for technical legitimacy, yet the framework is procedural.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger transparency requirements and conflict-of-interest rules.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>44. Federal criminal charging discretion.</strong> The practical power to decide what laws matter lies with executive prosecutorial discretion. Given the breadth of the federal code, the centrality of equal treatment, and the risk of selective enforcement, this is a weak structural arrangement.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>internal DOJ guidelines with statutory backing, and stronger congressional reporting requirements.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>45. FOIA and transparency.</strong> Public access to executive records is statutory, exception-ridden, and unevenly administered.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger superstatutory openness regime with presumption of disclosure, shorter response timelines, and meaningful penalties for noncompliance.</p></details>
</p>
</div>
</details>

<details class="mb-6 border border-base-border rounded-xl" open>
<summary class="cursor-pointer px-5 py-4 select-none">
<span class="text-xl font-semibold tracking-tight">VI. Judiciary, Continuity, and Regime Norms</span>
</summary>
<div class="px-5 pb-4">
<p>
These concern the courts, the civil-military boundary, and the unwritten preconditions of republican government.
</p>

<p>
<strong>46. Judicial review as constitutional settlement.</strong> The extent to which the Supreme Court functions as the final constitutional legislature on contested questions is a product of doctrine and political acceptance more than explicit textual command. This makes the settlement thinner than commonly assumed.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>better legislative constitutionalism (Congress writing clearer statutes) to reduce dependence on judicial settlement of major policy questions.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>47. Judicial impartiality and ethics.</strong> Article III provides life tenure and compensation protection, but day-to-day standards of impartiality rely on ethics rules and codes of conduct rather than more deeply entrenched architecture. Recent controversies have exposed the weakness.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>a binding, enforceable code of judicial ethics for all federal judges, including the Supreme Court, with an independent enforcement mechanism.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>48. Domestic military nonintervention.</strong> A liberal republic needs a firm boundary between military force and civilian policing. The Posse Comitatus principle is foundational in spirit, but the legal framework is statutory and riddled with exceptions.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>constitutional or strong superstatutory codification of the civilian-military boundary with narrower exceptions.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>49. Federal Reserve independence.</strong> Monetary authority is central to the modern state, yet the Fed's independence is statutory and customary.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>stronger superstatutory protection of Fed independence with clearer grounds for removal of governors.</p></details>
</p>

<p>
<strong>50. Constitutional good faith and institutional forbearance.</strong> The American system assumes officeholders will act within a shared constitutional culture rather than exploit every ambiguity. That assumption is unavoidable to some extent, but the degree to which the system relies on it remains striking. Once good-faith forbearance erodes, the legal architecture proves thinner than it appeared. Norm-dependence score: 4/4.
<details><summary>Reform →</summary><p>this is the hardest to address directly. The best approach is to reduce the system's dependence on good faith by strengthening the other 49 items on this list.</p></details>
</p>
</div>
</details>

<h2>Constitutional Maintenance</h2>

<p>
The United States is the world's oldest continuous constitutional democracy. It is also one of the hardest to amend. Article V requires two-thirds of both chambers of Congress plus three-fourths of state legislatures, a threshold so high that it has been met only 27 times in 237 years, and not once with a genuinely new structural idea since 1971. By comparison, Germany has amended its Basic Law over 60 times since 1949. France's Fifth Republic constitution has been amended 24 times since 1958. India amends its constitution roughly once a year. These are not unstable regimes. They are democracies that treat their constitutions as living infrastructure requiring maintenance, not sacred texts requiring veneration.
</p>

<p>
The American aversion to amendment has consequences. When the formal constitution cannot be updated, governance migrates to the places where change is possible: executive orders, judicial doctrines, agency rulemaking, and norms. Each of these is less durable than a constitutional settlement. Each is more vulnerable to reversal by the next administration, the next Court, or the next Congress. The result is a system that looks stable from the outside but is running on institutional habits that erode under stress.
</p>

<p>
None of the 50 items on this list requires a radical rethinking of American government. Most require only that the United States do what it claims to do: maintain a self-governing republic. That means updating the rules when they no longer match the conditions, strengthening the structures that bear the most weight, and reducing the system's dependence on the hope that every future officeholder will exercise self-restraint. Constitutional maintenance is not a threat to the founding vision. It is what the founding vision demands.
</p>
